LEGAL REASONING TRACE
JURISDICTIONAL SCRUTINY — This decision emanates from the Provincial Court of British Columbia (BCPC), the workhorse court for family law disputes under the Family Law Act. Judge Lamperson’s analysis is characteristically meticulous and statute-bound, reflecting the BCPC's mandate to apply the law efficiently within a high-volume docket. The reasoning demonstrates deep familiarity with the FLA’s relocation scheme but operates within a narrower analytical frame than a BCSC judgment might. There is no expansive discussion of common law principles outside the statutory text; the precedent cited (Hansen v. Ferguson) is from the BCSC and is used illustratively, not as binding authority. The precedential weight of this decision is limited to its persuasive value, but its utility lies in its exemplary, step-by-step application of the FLA’s complex relocation provisions—a template for practitioners.


STATUTORY SUBSUMPTION — The judgment is a masterclass in statutory application. The analysis is rigidly structured around the tripartite test in FLA s. 69(5) for guardians with substantially equal parenting time [para 10]. Each pillar is addressed in turn: 1) Good Faith (s. 69(6)): The judge meticulously evaluates the mother’s five reasons [para 51] against the four non-exhaustive factors (a) reasons, (b) enhancement of quality of life, (c) notice, (d) restrictions [paras 50-76]. 2) Reasonable & Workable Arrangements: The mother’s detailed proposed order [para 78] is scrutinized for its capacity to preserve the father-child relationship. 3) Best Interests (s. 37): A full, factor-by-factor analysis is conducted [paras 83-116]. The judge correctly applies s. 37(4), considering the father’s conduct (foul language, obstructing counseling) only insofar as it substantially affects a best interests factor, namely his ability to exercise parental responsibilities and the need for cooperation [paras 111-112, 115]. The post-relocation orders on parental responsibilities [paras 121-122] and communication [paras 123-125] further operationalize s. 40 and s. 225 of the FLA.

PRECEDENTIAL ALIGNMENT — The judge correctly channels the spirit of the SCC’s seminal relocation jurisprudence (Gordon v. Goertz) through the FLA’s distinct statutory lens. The statute modifies Gordon by creating specific procedural hurdles (good faith, proposed arrangements) before a full best interests analysis. The reference to Hansen v. Ferguson [para 88] is apt and reveals the strategic legal theory at play: the “Interconnected Well-Being Doctrine.” This theory, endorsed by the court, posits that the emotional and economic well-being of a child’s primary caregiver is inextricably linked to the child’s own best interests. The judge adopts this directly: “A.H.’s emotional well-being is inextricably tied to that of the mother” [para 90]. This aligns with the modern, child-centric approach mandated by Barendregt v. Grebliunas, which requires a holistic view of the child’s needs within their familial and social context. There is no precedent gap; the application is textbook.

ARGUMENT STRENGTH — DOCTRINE + EVIDENCE — The mother prevailed on the power of a comprehensive, evidence-based narrative that married adult self-improvement with indirect child benefit. Her lawyer’s theory was the “Interconnected Well-Being and Enhanced Stability” doctrine. She didn’t just argue her own desires; she demonstrated through corroborated evidence how her life improvement (family support, ending a grueling commute, financial savings, homeownership prospects) would create a more stable, enriched, and less stressed environment for A.H. [paras 52-73]. Critically, she paired this with a generous, detailed parenting plan [para 78] and testimony from M.W. affirming support for the father-child relationship [paras 41-42]. The father’s argument failed due to “The Static Status Quo Fallacy” and a self-focused narrative. His opposition was rooted in personal loss of weekday time and convenience, couched as concern for the relationship [para 47, 81]. He offered no alternative plan when challenged [para 80], and his own conduct (blocking counseling, delaying healthcare, refusing subsidies) undermined his claim to child-focused decision-making [paras 48, 63, 92-93]. His argument was legally deficient because it failed to engage with the statutory test constructively; it was mere opposition.

JUDICIAL SENTIMENT — The judgment radiates a controlled, professional frustration with the father’s obstructive and inconsistent stance. The disdain is not for the father as a parent but for his approach to the litigation and co-parenting. Key quotes reveal this: “I find it perplexing that he wishes to receive child tax benefits but has not applied for a child care subsidy” [para 63] – highlighting irrationality. “The father has stood in the way of her doing so... the father has objected...” [para 48] – framing him as an obstacle to the child’s needs. The observation that text messages show he “has become frustrated and angry and has resorted to using foul language” [para 111] paints a picture of poor conflict management. This cumulative portrait materially weakened his credibility on factors requiring cooperation and child-focused judgment.


IRAC/CoT SYNTHESIS
• ISSUE: Should the mother’s proposed relocation of the child, A.H., from Nanaimo to Port Alberni be permitted under s. 69 of the FLA? 
• RULE: For guardians with substantially equal parenting time (s. 69(5)), the relocating guardian must satisfy the court on a balance of probabilities that: (a) the relocation is proposed in good faith (considering factors in s.69(6)); (b) she has proposed reasonable and workable arrangements to preserve the child’s relationship with the other guardian; and (c) the relocation is in the child’s best interests (s.37). 
• APPLICATION: (a) Good Faith: The mother’s five reasons were found sincere, legitimate, and compelling [67, 77]. They addressed enhancement of quality of life [73], notice was proper [74], and no final order barred relocation [76]. (b) Reasonable Arrangements: The proposed schedule (3/4 weekends, half holidays, equal summers) with the mother/M.W. assuming travel burdens was found “both reasonable and workable” [82]. The father proposed no alternative [80]. (c) Best Interests: The s.37(2) analysis found multiple benefits: emotional well-being tied to mother’s enhanced stability [89-90]; strong preserved relationship with father [101]; greater stability from consistent school-week parenting and homeownership prospect [108, 110]; and the mother’s greater involvement in schooling/health decisions was preferable [86, 94, 112]. Detriments (less weekday time) were mitigated by generous parenting time. The father’s obstructive conduct negatively impacted factors (f) and (i) [111-112, 115]. 
• CONCLUSION: All three statutory burdens were met. Relocation permitted.
