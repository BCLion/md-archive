## INTRODUCTION

- [1] In this family law dispute, the claimant, MAJ, and the respondent, MEJ, seek a divorce from each other, and a final determination of division of property, parenting time and responsibilities, and spousal and child support.
- [2] MEJ was born January 24, 1965, in Beirut, Lebanon. He has a master's degree in engineering, but when he moved to the Lower Mainland in 2001, he found his credentials were not recognized. Instead, he found work as an elevator mechanic in 2003 at the age of 38 and has steadily and impressively gained experience and seniority in his new career. Only once he had established himself in Canada did he seek to start a family. At that time, he was introduced to MAJ, who was living in his home country of Lebanon at that time.
- [3] MAJ was born April 23, 1977, in Baghdad, Iraq. She was 25 years old when she moved to Lebanon in 2003 due to the Iraq war. She trained as a lawyer in Iraq and had worked as a lawyer for the government for almost two years when she moved to Lebanon with her whole family. There, she was introduced to and met with MEJ's sister and some of his other family members still living in Lebanon.
- [4] MAJ and MEJ spent several months speaking on the phone before they decided to marry. They met in person for the first time shortly before they were formally married in Syria, in May 2004. They then had a wedding ceremony in Beirut, Lebanon with their families in June 2004. They were 39 and 26 years old, respectively. After the wedding, MEJ returned to the Lower Mainland and worked to file all the paperwork to bring MAJ to Canada as his wife. MAJ arrived just over a year later in August 2005. Unfortunately, the evidence indicates that neither got what they had hoped and expected from this union.
- [5] The parties' first child, a son, was born approximately one year later, on August 17, 2006. Two girls followed, the first on January 1, 2011, and the second on May 28, 2014.

- [6] The parties separated on October 11, 2021, which MAJ says was a result of MEJ punching her. MEJ denies this and accuses MAJ of throwing objects at him. I will address these allegations further below. In any event, it is uncontested that the police were called, and MEJ left the family home on this date.
- [7] MEJ was 56 at the time of separation and was 60 years old at trial. He continues to work full time as an elevator mechanic. MAJ was 44 years old at the time of separation, and 47 years old at the time of trial. Before separation, MAJ had just completed her legal assistant accreditation, and had worked briefly in this role at the time of separation. She currently works minimal part time hours at her friend's spa as a receptionist.
- [8] The children were 7, 10, and 15 years old at the time of separation and 10, 14, and 18 years old at the time of the trial. Since separation, the children have primarily lived with MAJ, except for approximately one month when the eldest lived with MEJ in 2024. MEJ has had one weekend day a week parenting time with the two younger girls by consent order since May 4, 2022, though MEJ's exercise of this parenting time has been inconsistent. MEJ blames MAJ for this, while MAJ blames MEJ.
- [9] The eldest child graduated from high school last June and has worked full time as part of a trade apprenticeship organized with MEJ's assistance as an elevator mechanic. He will be turning 19 in August, and both parties agree that he will then no longer be a child of the marriage in need of parental support.
- [10] The middle child experienced a period of blindness last fall and has recently been diagnosed with childhood onset of Multiple Sclerosis ('MS'). This diagnosis is still new to the parties, but both are understandably concerned about their daughter's need for greater supports in the coming years.
- [11] MEJ was briefly represented by counsel at the Judicial Case Conference ('JCC') and early on in the litigation, but he was self-represented throughout most of the interim applications brought before this Court and at trial.

## ISSUES

- [12] Although I would characterize these proceedings as high conflict as a result of the parties' inability to effectively communicate, the parties' positions are not that far apart on many of the facts and legal issues before me at trial.
- [13] Both parties seek a divorce.
- [14] The parties agree that MAJ will continue to exercise primary parenting time with the children, which will allow MEJ to continue to work to support the family, as he has done throughout the marriage. They disagree, however, on whether MEJ should have more parenting time with the girls on the weekends, as MEJ seeks.
- [15] They also disagree about whether MEJ should be expected to maintain his pre-separation overtime hours post-separation as he enters his sixties for the purposes of support. They disagree also whether greater income should also be imputed to MAJ.
- [16] With respect to spousal support, MAJ's entitlement to spousal support is not challenged, but MEJ seeks to have it calculated on the basis of the lower income he has earned post-separation. He also seeks to have spousal support recalculated annually on the basis of each parties' income, rather than on a lump sum basis, as sought by MAJ.
- [17] Finally, the parties are generally agreed on the value of the main family assets worth over $2 million, but disagree on the value of some smaller assets and accounts. The primary issue with respect to property division is MAJ's claim for unequal division of the family assets, which MEJ opposes.
- [18] The issues that require resolution in this trial are:
8. (a) With respect to parenting:
- i. Should MEJ's parenting time be increased to include overnight parenting time on the weekends with the girls?

- ii. Is there a pattern of family violence that affects the best interests of the children in relation to parenting time and parenting responsibilities?
- iii.  Should the parties have shared parenting responsibilities, and if not, who should have final decision-making authority with respect to parenting responsibilities?
3. (b) With respect to support issues:
- i. What is MEJ's income available for child support purposes?
- ii. What is MAJ's income for support purposes, and should a higher income be imputed to her?
- iii.  At what scale and duration should MAJ receive spousal support?
- iv.  Should MAJ receive a lump sum spousal award?
- v.  Should there be retroactive accounting of the amount of support paid by MEJ since separation?
9. (c) With respect to the division of property:
- i. What is the total value of the family property and debt?
- ii. Should MAJ be awarded an unequal share family property pursuant to s. 95(3) of the Family Law Act , S.B.C. 2011, c. 25 [ FLA ] to allow her to retain the family home?
- [19] Before addressing these issues, I will briefly set out the procedural background and key events that provide context for the determinations below and briefly comment on the credibility and reliability issues before me at trial. I will also address the allegations of family violence in this case.

## CREDIBILITY

- [34] MEJ and MAJ each make significant allegations that the other is not to be believed by this Court. The parties challenge the reliability and credibility of the other, and the witnesses called by the other party. Many of these allegations were not directly relevant to the issues that I am required to resolve to settle the legal issues between the parties. Where relevant to a particular finding, I will address those allegations directly.
- [35] In making my findings of credibility and reliability, I keep in mind the guidance of this Court in Bradshaw v. Stenner , 2010 BCSC 1398, aff'd 2012 BCCA 296, leave to appeal to SCC ref'd, 35006 (7 March 2013), which discusses the oft-considered

factors that are utilized when assessing the credibility of witnesses, including that 'the real test of the truth of the story of a witness in such a case must be its harmony with the preponderance of the probabilities which a practical and informed person would readily recognize as reasonable in that place and in those conditions': Faryna v. Chorny , [1952] 2 D.L.R. 354 at 357, 1951 CanLII 252 (B.C.C.A.).

- [36] I also keep in mind the distinction between credibility and reliability, as explained by this Court in Hardychuk v. Johnstone , 2012 BCSC 1359:
- [10] The typical starting point in a credibility assessment is to presume truthfulness: Halteren . Truthfulness and reliability are not, however, necessarily the same. A witness may sincerely attempt to be truthful but lack the perceptive, recall or narrative capacity to provide reliable testimony. Alternatively, he or she may unconsciously indulge in the human tendency to reconstruct and distort history in a manner that favours a desired outcome. There is, of course, also the possibility that a witness may choose, consciously and deliberately, to lie out of perceived self-interest or for some other reason. Accordingly, when a witness's evidence is demonstrably inaccurate the challenge from an assessment perspective is to identify the likely reason for the inaccuracy in a cautious, balanced and contextually sensitive way.
- [37] I found both parties to have limitations in terms of both their credibility and reliability in their testimony before me. I cannot say that I consistently prefer the evidence of one party over the other. The same is true of their witnesses, who were generally strongly partisan in their evidence. Instead, I have considered the evidence as a whole on each issue upon which there is a factual dispute, and I have made my findings on the preponderance of the probabilities before me.

## FAMILY VIOLENCE

- [38] Section 1 of the FLA defines family violence as follows:
2. " family violence " includes, with or without an intent to harm a family member,
3. (a)  physical abuse of a family member, including forced confinement or deprivation of the necessities of life, but not including the use of reasonable force to protect oneself or others from harm,
4. (b)  sexual abuse of a family member,
5. (c)  attempts to physically or sexually abuse a family member,
6. (d)  psychological or emotional abuse of a family member, including

- (i) intimidation, harassment, coercion or threats, including threats respecting other persons, pets or property,
- (ii) unreasonable restrictions on, or prevention of, a family member's financial or personal autonomy,
- (iii) stalking or following of the family member, and
- (iv) intentional damage to property, and
- (e)  in the case of a child, direct or indirect exposure to family violence;
- [39] Section 38 of the FLA sets out required considerations in assessing the best interests of the children in relation to family violence:

## Assessing family violence

38 For the purposes of section 37 (2) (g) and (h) [ best interests of child ], a court must consider all of the following:

- (a)  the nature and seriousness of the family violence;
- (b)  how recently the family violence occurred;
- (c)  the frequency of the family violence;
- (d)  whether any psychological or emotional abuse constitutes, or is evidence of, a pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour directed at a family member;
- (e)  whether the family violence was directed toward the child;
- (f)  whether the child was exposed to family violence that was not directed toward the child;
- (g)  the harm to the child's physical, psychological and emotional safety, security and well-being as a result of the family violence;
- (h)  any steps the person responsible for the family violence has taken to prevent further family violence from occurring;
- (i) any other relevant matter.
- [40] The broad and inclusive language of these family violence provisions recognizes that harm to children may extend beyond physical violence: Morgadinho v. Morgadinho , 2014 BCSC 192 at para. 59. Furthermore, a single act of family violence may suffice to conclude a likelihood in the future: Dawson v. Dawson , 2014 BCSC 44 at para. 44.
- [41] Family violence need not be directed at a child to harm that child. In the recent decision of Barendregt v. Grebliunas , 2022 SCC 22, the Supreme Court of

Canada provided further guidance regarding the scope of family violence as it relates to parenting determinations:

[143] The suggestion that domestic abuse or family violence has no impact on the children and has nothing to do with the perpetrator's parenting ability is untenable. Research indicates that children who are exposed to family violence are at risk of emotional and behavioural problems throughout their lives: Department of Justice, Risk Factors for Children in Situations of Family Violence in the Context of Separation and Divorce (February 2014), at p. 12. Harm can result from direct or indirect exposure to domestic conflicts, for example, by observing the incident, experiencing its aftermath, or hearing about it: S. Artz et al., 'A Comprehensive Review of the Literature on the Impact of Exposure to Intimate Partner Violence for Children and Youth' (2014), 5 I.J.C.Y.F.S. 493, at p. 497.

[42] In some cases, a pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour is sufficient to find that family violence has occurred and to consider its impact on the safety and well-being of the children involved. For example, in R.C.T. v. A.K.T. , 2023 BCSC 654, Justice Donegan found that a pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour, including threats of physical force, demeaning remarks, and abusive communications, both before and after separation, constituted psychological and emotional abuse: at paras. 175-178.

[43] Before me in this trial are allegations by both parties of physical violence. MAJ also alleges that MEJ exercised a level of financial control over her that was abusive throughout the marriage, and stalking behaviour after separation.

[44] I will deal first with MEJ's allegations that MAJ would hurt and scratch him, which he gave little evidence about, but which he says was the subject of various police reports during the marriage. I find the trial evidence does not establish MEJ's allegations in this respect.

[45] The only evidence of police involvement in this marriage before me at trial was the involvement of the police on the separation date, October 11, 2021, when the police escorted MEJ from the Family Home. Neither party sought to introduce the police reports or investigations with respect to that intervention, including what allegations of violence were made by each of the parties of that time. MEJ admits that he was charged but says the charges were stayed or dropped, and there is no

evidence that they proceeded. I infer from the trial evidence that some sort of peace bond or other no contact provisions were in place between the parties for several months after the separation.

[46] MAJ says that this incident was the result of MEJ punching her in the stomach, threatening to sexually assault her, and threatening to slash her tires if she tried to take the car to see her friends. MEJ denies punching MAJ and says that it was MEJ who threw a heavy object at him that could have hurt him badly but did not. He agrees he threatened to slash her tires but says he never would have actually done so because he would have been responsible for fixing them. He thought this was funny. He also admitted to blocking her ability to leave the garage that morning by driving his work car across the driveway.

[47] MEJ also admitted to threatening to sexually assault MAJ and to sending MAJ a text message demanding certain sex acts for money the morning of the separation. He says that MAJ would regularly demand money from him for acts of intimacy, but that this was an example of him demanding acts that she would never agree to because he did not want to pay her. The money for which he demanded the sex acts related to a grocery bill for items MAJ purchased to cook a Thanksgiving dinner the previous night for the family and two other families that joined them. MEJ's testimony was that the fight was about MAJ refusing to cook a similar meal for his friends.

[48] I find that this was the genesis of the fight that occurred the morning of the separation-a fight about MAJ's spending money on groceries and MEJ's pattern of controlling and negotiating what he would pay for and what he would not. I find that this pattern was not unusual, and that MEJ exerted a high degree of control over MAJ's spending throughout the marriage. The evidence establishes that MEJ provided MAJ with a monthly allowance and a credit card with a fixed limit for groceries and other items for herself, the children and the family, which she considered inadequate, and he considered generous. Throughout the marriage, MEJ

would regularly review MAJ's expenses, including gas and specific grocery items, and question MAJ on the necessity for various items.

[49] Setting aside for the moment MAJ's allegations of two incidents of physical violence, one on the day of separation and one 16 days earlier, which are categorically denied by MEJ, I find that the largely uncontroverted evidence at trial establishes an ongoing pattern of coercion and control exercised by MEJ over MAJ, in terms of her finances and where she could go.

[50] I find that this pattern did not extend to restricting MAJ from working outside the home and earning money. MEJ clearly supported MAJ to improve her English, pursue her education, learn to drive, and wanted her to contribute financially to the family expenses. However, I find that MEJ's expectation was that this would be done without sacrificing her primary role as mother and wife, which involved primary care of the children and home cooked meals, three meals a day when the family was home. I accept MAJ's evidence that she never felt adequate in this regard, and that MEJ would deride her cooking and her performance of her duties to their friends.

[51] Overall, I find that MAJ has established a level of coercion and control in the marriage that amounts to family violence under the FLA .

[52] Furthermore, I find that MEJ's controlling behaviours extended beyond separation. MEJ continued to attempt to monitor MAJ, including her comings and goings at the Family Home through monitoring the parties' home security system, and her places of work where he would appear, and video tape her.

[53] In light of the seriousness of the above findings and the limitations noted above with respect to the testimonial evidence of the parties, I decline to make any further factual determinations with respect to MAJ's strongly contested allegations of specific acts of physical violence in the three weeks leading up to separation. These further allegations of physical violence would not affect or change my finding that family violence is a significant factor in this case, and must be considered in relation to the parenting and property division issues before me.

[54] I am also satisfied that any ongoing risk of physical violence between MEJ and MAJ is now low, and will be further reduced by the achievement of some finality in the parties financial and parenting arrangements.

## PARENTING

- [56] As set out above, the only parenting issue before me for decision is whether to grant MEJ additional parenting time on weekends as he seeks.
- [57] Since May 2022, MEJ has had ordered parenting time with the girls one day each weekend from 10am to 4pm. MEJ says that he has not been able to exercise this parenting time consistently, including a period of time between July 2022 and February 2023 when he had no parenting time with the children at all, until the 2023 Consent Order. Since then, MEJ says his parenting time has been inconsistent, and he points to a time where the girls went to a birthday party at a mall and he waited for them to come to him, and they never did. He says that their middle child often makes excuses and does not want to see him.
- [58] Overall, the evidence was unclear on how often MEJ was able to exercise his court ordered parenting time, and when he has not. MEJ blames MAJ for alienating the girls against him, and his sister testified that she strongly believes this alienation is occurring based on a conversation she heard between MAJ and the middle child in early 2022 when the child was 11 years old.
- [59] For her part, MAJ says that she does not interfere with the children's relationship with their father and indeed encourages it. She says, however, that the children themselves choose when and if they see their father for the most part, and it

is not her fault if they choose not to. She suggests that it is MEJ's own controlling behaviours that are alienating the children from him. In any event, the children have always primarily relied upon her for parental support, and that this has not changed since separation.

- [60] It is well established that the best interests of the children are the sole consideration of this Court when considering a parenting regime under the FLA .
- [61] The best interests of the child are defined in s. 37 of the FLA :

## Best interests of child

- 37 (1) In making an agreement or order under this Part respecting guardianship, parenting arrangements or contact with a child, the parties and the court must consider the best interests of the child only.
- (2) To determine what is in the best interests of a child, all of the child's needs and circumstances must be considered, including the following:
- (a) the child's health and emotional well-being;
- (b) the child's views, unless it would be inappropriate to consider them;
- (c) the nature and strength of the relationships between the child and significant persons in the child's life;
- (d) the history of the child's care;
- (e) the child's need for stability, given the child's age and stage of development;
- (f) the ability of each person who is a guardian or seeks guardianship of the child, or who has or seeks parental responsibilities, parenting time or contact with the child, to exercise his or her responsibilities;
- (g) the impact of any family violence on the child's safety, security or wellbeing, whether the family violence is directed toward the child or another family member;
- (h) whether the actions of a person responsible for family violence indicate that the person may be impaired in his or her ability to care for the child and meet the child's needs;
- (i) the appropriateness of an arrangement that would require the child's guardians to cooperate on issues affecting the child, including whether requiring cooperation would increase any risks to the safety, security or wellbeing of the child or other family members;
- (j) any civil or criminal proceeding relevant to the child's safety, security or well-being.
- (3) An agreement or order is not in the best interests of a child unless it protects, to the greatest extent possible, the child's physical, psychological and emotional safety, security and well-being.

- (4) In making an order under this Part, a court may consider a person's conduct only if it substantially affects a factor set out in subsection (2), and only to the extent that it affects that factor.
- [62] With respect to parenting arrangements and responsibilities, s. 40 of the FLA provides that:

## Parenting arrangements

- 40 (1) Only a guardian may have parental responsibilities and parenting time with respect to a child.
- (2) Unless an agreement or order allocates parental responsibilities differently, each child's guardian may exercise all parental responsibilities with respect to the child in consultation with the child's other guardians, unless consultation would be unreasonable or inappropriate in the circumstances.
- (3) Parental responsibilities may be allocated under an agreement or order such that they may be exercised by
- (a)  one or more guardians only, or
- (b)  each guardian acting separately or all guardians acting together.
- (4) In the making of parenting arrangements, no particular arrangement is presumed to be in the best interests of the child and without limiting that, the following must not be presumed:

that parental responsibilities should be allocated equally among guardians;

- (a)  that parenting time should be shared equally among guardians;
- (b)  that decisions among guardians should be made separately or together.

## Legal Principles regarding the Determination of Income for Support Purposes

[100] The Supreme Court of Canada's decision in D.B.S. v. S.R.G. , 2006 SCC 37 identifies the following core principles underlying child support obligations (at paras. 38, 107):

- a)  Child support is the right of the child;
- b)  The right to support survives the breakdown of a child's parents' marriage;
- c)  Child support should, as much as possible, provide children with the same standard of living they enjoyed when their parents were together; and
- d)  The specific amounts of child support owed will vary based on the income of the payor parent.
- [101] A payor parent who knowingly avoids or diminishes their support obligation to their children should not be allowed to profit from that conduct: D.B.S. at para. 107.

[102] The calculation of a spouse's income for child support purposes is governed by ss. 16-20 of the Guidelines . The starting point is that a party's annual income for support purposes is determined using the sources of income set out under the heading 'Total Income' at Line 15000 of the CRA T1 general form.

[103] However, where the court is of the view that the Line 15000 income 'would not be the fairest determination of that income,' then 'the court may have regard to the spouse's income over the last three years and determine an amount that is fair and reasonable in light of any pattern of income, fluctuation in income, or receipt of a non-recurring amount during those years': Guidelines , s. 17(1). Often, the court will average those three years, but s. 17(1) provides the court with broad discretion to consider a greater span of years, or to find that some years are more reliably indicative of the amounts available to that spouse to pay support obligations: Reid v. Reid , 2017 BCCA 73 at para. 122, citing Harras v. Lhotka , 2016 BCCA 246 at para. 21. While this discretion is broad, 'it is not to be exercised arbitrarily': Reid at para. 122.

## Spousal Support

- [139] Section 15.2 of the Divorce Act governs entitlement to spousal support. Subsection 15.2(6) provides that a spousal support order should meet the following objectives:
2. (a) recognize any economic advantages or disadvantages to the spouses arising from the marriage or its breakdown;
3. (b) apportion between the spouses any financial consequences arising from the care of any child of the marriage over and above any obligation for the support of any child of the marriage;
4. (c) relieve any economic hardship of the spouses arising from the breakdown of the marriage; and
5. (d) in so far as practicable, promote the economic self-sufficiency of each spouse within a reasonable period of time.

- [140] These objectives are also mirrored in the language of s. 161 of the FLA .
- [141] In order to achieve a fair and equitable distribution of the economic consequences of marriage and marriage breakdown, all four of the objectives must be taken into account: Moge v. Moge , [1992] 3 S.C.R. 813 at 850-853, 1992 CanLII 25. Having regard to these objectives, courts must consider the condition, means, needs and other circumstances of each spouse, including the factors set out in s. 15.2(4):
3. (a) the length of time the spouses cohabited;
4. (b) the functions performed by each spouse during cohabitation; and
5. (c) any order, agreement or arrangement relating to support of either.
- [142] Consistent with the objectives, our courts have recognized three grounds for entitlement to spousal support: compensatory, non-compensatory and contractual: Bracklow v. Bracklow , [1999] 1 S.C.R. 420 at para. 49, 1999 CanLII 715; and Chutter v. Chutter , 2008 BCCA 507, leave to appeal to SCC ref'd, 33002 (28 May 2009). A court is not called upon to decide on one basis for support to the exclusion of the other but rather to '[apply] the relevant factors and strik[e] the balance that best achieves justice in the particular case': Chutter at para. 49, citing Bracklow at para. 32. Moreover, the doctrine of equitable sharing is the overarching principle that must be borne in mind: Chutter at para. 49, citing Moge at 864.
- [143] MAJ claims spousal support from MEJ on both a compensatory and a noncompensatory basis, as outlined in Chutter . MAJ seeks a substantial lump sum spousal support award together with a 55% reapportionment of family property to address what she argues is a strong compensatory claim which MEJ will be unable to fulfill on a periodic basis given his potential for retirement. Alternatively, she seeks a substantial 65% reapportionment of family property pursuant to s. 95(3) of the FLA to address her ongoing compensatory claim.

[144] MEJ does not contest MAJ's entitlement to spousal support but argues that support should be on the basis of his current income not his pre-separation income, and adjusted annually rather than paid as a lump sum or through property reapportionment.

[145] I find that this marriage, where MAJ was assigned and performed the role of raising three children, shopping for and preparing home made meals, and maintaining the home, engages the requirement of compensatory spousal support pursuant to the principles set out in Chutter . I note that it is not necessary for MAJ to prove on a balance of probabilities the existence of any particular loss of opportunity she has suffered: Chutter at para. 67.

[146] Once a party has established entitlement to support, the Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines (' SSAG ') should generally be followed for the purposes of quantifying that support. Exceptional circumstances may be required to justify a substantial departure from the SSAG : Sebok v. Babits , 2022 BCCA 2 at para. 24.

[147] With respect to non-compensatory grounds, the evidence establishes a stark disparity between MAJ 's income post-separation and that of MEJ. MEJ's efforts to build his career throughout the marriage and to earn income for the family, would not have been possible without the assistance of MAJ managing their home and being primarily responsible for the children, including when MEJ would return tired from work at the end of each workday, including on weekends. This investment by both parties has left MEJ with job security and a well-paying job, but the same cannot be said for MAJ. While I find the MEJ encouraged MAJ to improve her English and pursue part-time studies to supplement the family income, I find that she was expected to do so without compromise to her primary role as a mother and wife. I therefore find that she is also entitled to spousal support on a non-compensatory basis.

[148] Chapter 9 of the SSAG provides an overview of factors which may be considered in determining where spousal support should fall within the applicable range. As noted in the SSAG, no single factor is determinative, and several factors

may be at play in any given case. I consider the following factors to be significant in this case:

- (a) Section 9.1: A strong compensatory claim is a factor that favours a support award at the higher end of the ranges for amount and duration;
- (b) Section 9.2: When a recipient has limited income and/or earning capacity their needs may push an award to the higher end of the range for spousal support; and
- (c) Section 9.3: A child with special needs will usually demand more time and resources from the care-giving parent, thus reducing that parent's ability to earn in the paid labour market and pushing spousal support towards the upper end.

[149] In my view, these factors support an award at the higher end of the range. MAJ is younger than MEJ and is qualified to work as a legal assistant. However, she is also limited by her proficiency in English which, while good, is likely an obstacle in professions that need strong English skills. She is also limited by her lack of work history approaching her fifties, and her need for a flexible workplace that allows her to change positions as she works. I also recognize that, as the primary caregiver, MAJ may require heightened flexibility from her employer, particularly regarding the middle child's recent diagnosis with MS, and that this demand may increase over time. Given the length of this marriage, and that MAJ will continue to be the primary caregiver to the youngest child for another decade, and to the middle child for a potentially longer period given her diagnosis, I also consider that MAJ will continue to have family demands on her time and her ability to become self-sufficient that are not present for MEJ.

[150] Considering the above factors and objectives, I would award MAJ spousal support at the higher end of the spectrum.

[151] With respect to the duration of that support, the SSAG would suggest indefinite support given the length of this marriage. There is also a case to be made

for indefinite support given the unknown future impact of MAJ being the primary caregiver of the middle child beyond her teenage years. Recently, in Szeto v. Szeto , 2025 BCSC 914, this Court observed that spousal support may be the most appropriate mechanism under the FLA to address the long term sacrifices that a parent with primary care-giving responsibilities may incur where a child of the marriage needs support due to a disability into the future. This makes a spousal support award that reflects the potential for this sacrifice to continue for an indefinite period also appropriate.

[152] However, given that MAJ seeks a lump sum spousal support in this case, additional considerations must be made in relation to the duration of support in the context of this request, as a lump sum award removes the ability to readjust spousal support based on changing income and contingencies on an indefinite basis.

## Lump Sum Support

- [153] This Court and the Court of Appeal have held in several cases that lump sum spousal support is appropriate where there has been previous disregard of a support order, where there is a concern that the payor will not pay or be able to pay periodic support, or where there is a high level of conflict between the parties; see e.g., Foster v. Foster , 2007 BCCA 83 at paras. 15, 53, 58; and Zhang v. Sun , 2016 BCSC 1418 at paras. 302-306. All of these factors apply in this case.
- [154] In Parton v. Parton , 2018 BCCA 273 the Court of Appeal set out certain considerations with respect to its determination of an appropriate lump sum spousal support award of $388,591 in that case. More recently, in R.L. v. M.L. , 2024 BCSC 106, Justice MacNaughton summarized the law pertaining to lump sum awards, including the circumstances in which lump sum support may be awarded:
- [241] The advantages and disadvantages of lump sum spousal support were discussed in Davis v. Crawford , 2011 ONCA 294 [ Davis ]:
- [67] The advantages of making [a lump sum] award will be highly variable and case-specific. They can include but are not limited to terminating ongoing contact or ties between the spouses for any number of reasons (for example, short-term marriage; domestic violence; second marriage with no children, etc.); providing capital to

meet an immediate need on the part of a dependent spouse; ensuring adequate support will be paid in circumstances where there is a real risk of non-payment of periodic support, a lack of proper financial disclosure or where the payor has the ability to pay lump sum but not periodic support; and satisfying immediately an award of retroactive spousal support.

[68] Similarly, the disadvantages of such an award can include the real possibility that the means and needs of the parties will change over time, leading to the need for a variation; the fact that the parties will be effectively deprived of the right to apply for a variation of the lump sum award; and the difficulties inherent in calculating an appropriate award of lump sum spousal support where lump sum support is awarded in place of ongoing indefinite periodic support.

See also Parton v. Parton , 2018 BCCA 273 at para. 27 and Robinson v. Robinson, 2011 BCSC 1489, aff'd 2012 BCCA 497.

[242] In Davis , the Ontario Court of Appeal went on to discuss the four practical reasons why most spousal support orders will be for periodic payments rather than lump sum amounts: see paras. 70-74. Those reasons are: the inability to fund a lump sum; property equalization payments may obviate the need for transitional capital to be available to a dependent spouse; there may not be any considerations favouring a lump sum form the perspective of either spouse; and the general exigencies of life, including the possibility that a parties' means and needs will change, may outweigh the considerations favouring a lump sum award.

[243] Recently in Zilic v. Zilic , 2021 BCCA 107, the Court of Appeal accepted the analysis in Davis and upheld a lump sum award, noting that such an award is appropriate where, amongst other reasons, there is a real risk of non-payment should monthly support be ordered and there is a need for the recipient spouse to become self-sufficient and a lump sum may better assist in that regard: see paras. 24, 207-209. Specifically, the Court stated:

[207] A number of factors were relevant in this case: the need for the recipient spouse to become self-sufficient and the ability of a lump sum to better assist in that regard; the ability of the payor spouse to use capital to pay the award; a high-conflict relationship; and the real risk of non-payment in the future should monthly support be ordered, given the track record of the payor spouse.



[209] The judge cannot be said to be clearly wrong in considering it more reasonable to order a lump-sum spousal support award, based on a limited duration, rather than to leave open the likelihood of future ongoing litigation about support payments. This is especially so where the lump-sum amount, while significant to the parties, could easily be exceeded by legal fees if there was ongoing litigation. The historical power dynamics between the two parties, where Mr. Zilic had the benefit of more income to fund legal fees and more incentive to be difficult in revealing the details of his actual self-employment income, also made a lump-sum award appropriate.

[Emphasis added.]

- [155] There are several factors that indicate that a lump sum spousal award is appropriate in this case, including:
2. (a) The history of family violence and in particular a pattern of controlling behaviour by MEJ regarding MAJ's financial decision making;
3. (b) A history of MEJ acting unilaterally to reduce his court-ordered support obligations;
4. (c) A history of many pre-trial applications regarding financial issues;
5. (d) The greater ability of MEJ to satisfy a lump sum spousal support award through the division of assets, thereby avoiding any incentive for MEJ to reduce his income post-trial similar to what occurred post-separation, or to retire earlier than originally expected as he has threatened in the past.

[156] I also consider that a lump sum award in this case also better serves the goals of spousal support in allowing MAJ to become self-sufficient. To the extent that it may allow her to purchase MEJ's interest in the Family Home, it may also provide an ongoing source of income through rental of the suites, well past MEJ's ability to support both MAJ and the children due to his retirement, which may well come before either of the girls are out of their mother's care.

[157] At the same time, I must consider that a lump sum award, although discounted for its net present value, will not necessarily capture changes to the parties' incomes, including MEJ's eventual retirement and the positive contingencies of MAJ increasing her income beyond what I have imputed to her.

- [158] Taking into account the various contingencies, I have considered various potential lump sum awards using the DivorceMate software at the midpoint of the net present value and after-tax impact to each party, for both shorter terms and for indefinite terms on the basis set out above, and in scenarios where MEJ's income decreases and MAJ's income increases over the next ten years.

[159] Taking the above into consideration, I find that MAJ is entitled to a lump sum award of spousal support in the amount of $300,000. This award is at the low end of the spousal support range for an indefinite period at the incomes I have imputed to the parties, and at the mid-range should MAJ's income increase substantially over the next 10 years but MEJ's income remain where I have imputed it. An award of $300,000 would also reflect the high end of a shorter duration of spousal support.

## DIVISION OF FAMILY PROPERTY

## Statutory Framework

[179] Both parties seek a division of family property pursuant to the FLA . Section 81 of the FLA provides that on the date of separation, each spouse is entitled to an

undivided half interest in all family property and is equally responsible for family debt regardless of their use or contribution to it.

- [180] Family property is defined in s. 84(1)(a) of the FLA as all real and personal property that is owned by either spouse on the date of separation, unless the property is excluded from family property under s. 85(1). Any real or personal property owned by at least one spouse at the time of separation is family property. The value of the family property is generally the fair market value at the date of the trial, subject to agreement or as otherwise ordered: FLA s. 87; D.Z. v. M.Z. , 2022 BCSC 706 at para. 152.
- [181] Where property is established to be family property, exclusions may apply pursuant to the FLA , including amounts contributed solely by one spouse. Although the original value of excluded property is not family property, any increase in its value from the time of the marriage to the time of the trial is family property: FLA s. 84(2)(g).
- [182] Family debt is defined by s. 86(a) of the FLA as including all financial obligations incurred by a spouse during the period of the relationship. Debt incurred after the date of separation is only family debt if it is incurred for the purpose of maintaining family property: FLA s. 86(b).
- [183] Under s. 87(b)(ii) of the FLA , the presumptive date for valuing family property and debt is the date of the trial. In most cases, departing from the presumptive date of valuation is effectively a reapportionment of family property and debt, which can only be achieved through an application of s. 95 of the FLA and a finding that an equal division would result in 'significant unfairness': Kumagai v. Campbell Estate , 2018 BCCA 24 at para. 76; see also Namdarpour v. Vahman , 2019 BCCA 153 at paras. 52, 62.
- [184] The court may consider the various factors in ordering an unequal division of family property and debt under s. 95(2) of the FLA, including:

- (c) a spouse's contribution to the career or career potential of the other spouse;

...

- (f) whether a spouse, after the date of separation, caused a significant decrease or increase in the value of family property or family debt beyond market trends;
- [185] The parties' relative contributions to family assets and debts during the marriage is largely irrelevant to reapportionment under s. 95(2) of the FLA . Although the FLA does not preclude relative contributions as a factor that might be considered under s. 95(2)(i) as one of 'any other factor[s]  that may lead to significant unfairness,' the circumstances in which it may be considered and relied on are now substantially constrained. It is for this reason that I have not considered evidence advanced by MEJ about his contributions to MAJ's father's business during the course of the marriage, or other evidence directed towards MAJ's spending during the marriage (which I note was tightly controlled by MEJ in any event).
- [186] In Jaszczewska v. Kostanski , 2016 BCCA 286, the Court of Appeal distinguished the approach to be taken under the FLA from its predecessor:
- [43] The determination of whether an equal division would be significantly unfair is also guided by a more precise identification of relevant factors than was found in the FRA. Importantly, the Legislature did not include in s. 95(2) the principle of relative contribution found in s. 65(1) of the FRA. Under s. 65(1)(f), a court could consider circumstances relating to the acquisition, preservation, maintenance, improvement or use of property. Section 95(2), by comparison, refers rather narrowly to career contributions (95(2)(c) and to post-separation increases in value beyond market trends caused by one spouse (95(s)(f)). I agree with the judge's view that if relative contribution to the acquisition, preservation, maintenance or improvement of family property during the relationship was intended to be a significant factor or one frequently relied on in justifying the conclusion that the equal division of family property is significantly unfair, the Legislature would have said so. Allowing relative contribution to become a regular consideration in the context of s. 95 would likely create uncertainty and complexity. This would be contrary to the legislative objectives discussed earlier that underlie the FLA division of property regime.
- [44] Having said that, in enacting s. 95(2)(i) the Legislature recognized that there may be factors other than those listed that could ground significant unfairness. Hence, while the Legislature intended to limit and constrain the exercise of judicial discretion to depart from equal division, it did not provide a closed list of factors and it did not eliminate the discretion. Accordingly, in my

view, one cannot read the FLA as abolishing unequal contribution as a factor that may be relevant to reapportionment, although the circumstances in which it may be considered and relied on are intended to be much constrained.

[187] Section 95(3) of the FLA provides that the court may also consider the extent to which the financial means and earning capacity of a spouse have been affected by the responsibilities and other circumstances of the relationship between the spouses if, on making a determination respecting spousal support, the objectives of spousal support under s. 161-as discussed earlier in these Reasons-have not been met.

- [188] In N.D.S. v J.A.S ., 2020 BCSC 1034, Justice Riley considered s. 95(3):
- [77] This brings me directly to s. 95(3), under which the Court must consider the extent to which the financial means and earning capacity of a spouse have been affected by the responsibilities and other circumstances of the relationship. If a spousal support order is contemplated, but the objectives in s. 161 of the FLA have not been met by that spousal support order, this may result in or contribute to a finding of significant unfairness so as to justify an unequal division of family property.
- [189] In N.K. v. M.H. , 2020 BCCA 121 the Court of Appeal set out the following guidance respecting the application of s. 95(3):
- [74] Where a spousal support order is insufficient to meet the objectives of spousal support, s. 95(3) of the FLA provides that this is a factor in assessing a claim for an unequal division of property. Furthermore, in Moge. v. Moge , [1992] 3 SCR 813 (S.C.C.), which dealt with an application to vary a support order made at the time of divorce, Justice L'Heureux-Dub made the following oft-cited statement at 848-849:

[T]he purpose of spousal support is to relieve economic hardship that results from 'marriage or its breakdown'. Whatever the respective advantages to the parties of a marriage in other areas, the focus of the inquiry when assessing spousal support after the marriage has ended must be the effect of the marriage in either impairing or improving each party's economic prospects.



Equitable distribution can be achieved in many ways: by spousal and child support, by the division of property and assets or by a combination of property and support entitlements.

[Emphasis in original.]

[75] As this passage illustrates, a range of remedies is available to relieve the economic hardship of the relationship or its breakdown.
