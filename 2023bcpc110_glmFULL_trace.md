LEGAL REASONING TRACE
In this protracted 17-day trial before the BC Provincial Court, Judge Brecknell navigates a thicket of high-conflict litigation involving a teenager, B., diagnosed with high-functioning Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), OCD, and severe anxiety. The respondent father, C.G.H., sought a dramatic shift in the regime—equal parenting time, sole guardianship, and final decision-making—relying on a Section 211 report by Dr. Aube that diagnosed parental alienation. The applicant mother, S.T.M., resisted, arguing B.’s refusal was rooted in estrangement due to C.G.H.’s historical absences and B.’s neurodivergence.

The Court’s jurisdictional scrutiny here is surgical. Judge Brecknell applies Faryna and Bradshaw to assess credibility, finding both parties wanting: S.T.M. is a "campaigning witness" prone to exaggeration; C.G.H. is "evasive" and resentful. Crucially, the Judge engages in a rigorous statutory analysis under the Family Law Act (FLA), particularly s. 37 (best interests of the child) and s. 211 (hearings).

On the critical issue of parental alienation, the Judge distinguishes between the concepts defined in Williamson v. Williamson and N.R.G. v. N.R.G. The Court rejects the "parental alienation" theory espoused by Dr. Aube and C.G.H. Instead, it finds B. is "estranged" from her father due to his early absences for fieldwork and education, combined with S.T.M.’s "enmeshment." While S.T.M. is criticized for reinforcing B.’s dependency (making her the "point person"), the Court stops short of finding the malicious intent required for a finding of family violence. This is a devastating blow to C.G.H.’s primary legal theory, effectively shielding S.T.M. from the "alienator" label.

Regarding parenting time, the Court applies Gordon v. Goertz principles—specifically the child's need for stability and routine given her ASD. B.’s views are given "less weight" because they are filtered through her enmeshment with S.T.M. The Court refuses C.G.H.’s demand for immediate equal time, calling it a "negative and catastrophic result" that would disrupt B.’s fragile mental health. The Court does, however, find S.T.M. "wrongfully denied" parenting time on Sundays, applying K.R. v. K.R. to hold S.T.M. accountable for failing to facilitate B.’s attendance. This results in a compensatory order for C.G.H.

Financially, the case turns on the mechanics of income attribution. The Judge applies Hausmann principles to C.G.H.’s closely held corporation, [omitted]. C.G.H.’s attempt to write off personal expenses like a Porsche Carrera and home renovations is rejected as "personal benefit of the shareholder." The Court imputes significant income to C.G.H., boosting his guideline income to ~$200,000 for support purposes. The Court also applies A.L.P. regarding S.T.M.’s Veterans Affairs pension, treating it as means available for support.

The Judge exercises his gatekeeper function regarding the Section 211 report (M.F.W. v. M.A.H.). While Dr. Aube’s report criticized S.T.M., the Judge finds it flawed—relying on undisclosed documents and failing to account for B.’s ASD diagnosis. Consequently, the recommendations for C.G.H. to have sole decision-making authority are dismissed. Instead, the Court orders a "Joyce model" for parental responsibilities, granting S.T.M. final decision-making power but requiring prior consultation.

Ultimately, the decision prioritizes B.’s immediate psychological safety over abstract ideals of "maximum contact." The status quo—with C.G.H. at ~35% time—is preserved, bolstered by compensatory time for missed Sundays and a mandatory Parenting Coordinator to manage the high-conflict dynamic. It is a classic judicial split: Mom keeps primary residence and final say; Dad gets his time increased (technically) and his support obligations met; both are sanctioned for their conduct.
