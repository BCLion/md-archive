## INTRODUCTION

- [1] This is a high-conflict  family law dispute. The issues to be determined include entitlement  to divorce, parenting arrangements  for the five children of the marriage, as well as child and spousal support. Each issue will be addressed separately.

## CASE MANAGEMENT AND SEIZURE OF FUTURE APPLICATIONS

[48] The term 'high  conflict' fails to properly capture the depth of animosity displayed by the parties in this case. To date, there have been countless applications by the parties resulting in 21 orders issued by 10 different judges in the Supreme and Provincial Courts. I am the 11th member of the judiciary to handle this matter and, unlike  my predecessors, I have  had the benefit of 17 days of evidence from 26 different witnesses.

[49] I  am in complete agreement with the comments  of Madam Justice Martinson in A.A. v. S.N.A. , 2009 BCSC 387, to the following effect:

77. It is imperative in high conflict family cases generally, and certainly in cases involving allegations of alienation like this one, that one member of the Court take charge of the case. Having a single judge hear cases is required by s. 14 of the Supreme Court Act :
- (1) All proceedings in the court and all business arising from those proceedings, if practicable and convenient, must be heard, determined and disposed of before a single judge.
- (2) All proceedings subsequent to the hearing or trial including the final order, except as otherwise provided, and on a rehearing must, if practicable and convenient, be before the judge before whom the trial or hearing took place.
78. The reasons for doing so for all cases are obvious. The judge will be familiar with the case so the litigants do not have to explain the situation over and over again. It avoids 'judge shopping' to try to get a better result. It prevents inconsistent approaches. It saves legal and other costs. There will be times in dealing with some cases when it is not convenient or practical to do so.
79. However, in high conflict custody cases the stakes are particularly high; children can be seriously harmed by the ongoing acrimony and lack of a timely resolution. It should be only in rare circumstances that the court finds it inconvenient or impractical to have a single judge hear the applications in cases of this sort.
80. It is common for these cases to come before the court numerous times before the trial takes place. The problems do not end with the trial judgment. After the trial, it is equally common to have one parent or both make repeated applications to the court, often trying to change the result at trial. Issues that have been decided at trial are re-argued. Highly contentious access problems arise. There are allegations of breaches of court orders. These problems can, if unchecked, go on for many months or even years. It is the trial judge who knows the situation; that judge, in my opinion, should

deal with the matter as it continues to unfold, as envisioned by s. 14 of the Supreme Court Act .

81. Unless a particular judge decides to become seized of a case, the applications can be heard by many different judges. I was the fifth judge to deal with this case after the Court of Appeal made its decision in June 2007. If a judge does become seized of a case, the applications normally must be dealt with on top of the judge's regular workload, before or after normal court hours. This ad hoc approach is not effective. It does not give these important cases, and the children involved in them, the attention they deserve.
82. In my respectful view, an institutional response is required which identifies these cases early and ensures that they are dealt with by one judge who has experience in and an interest in family law. The importance of effective case management in high conflict cases is supported by judges, legal academics, medical professionals, and lawyers.

[50] While the institutional  response referred to by Madam Justice Martinson has not yet occurred, it is nonetheless  imperative and in the best interests of the children,  the parents and the court for one member of this Court to be seized of any further  applications that may be made in this case. I will therefore remain seized of all such  further  applications. However,  in the case of any application involving urgency  and where, for any reason, I am not available to hear the matter, such urgent  application can be heard by another  member of this Court.

## Psychologist and Counsellor Witnesses

## Dr. Peter Meuser

[198] On July  16, 2013, this Court ordered the preparation of a report pursuant  to s. 211(1) of the Family Law  Act by a psychologist to be selected jointly  by both parties. This was not a consent order. The application had been opposed by the mother.

[199] The parties eventually  agreed to retain Dr. Peter Meuser for this assessment. The joint retainer letter signed by counsel for both parties was issued to Dr. Meuser on September 11, 2013.

[200] Dr. Meuser has been a registered clinical psychologist  in British Columbia since 1980. He obtained his Ph.D. in clinical psychology  at the University  of Ottawa in 1978. He testified that for the last 10 years his practice has been devoted almost exclusively  to psychological assessments for the purposes of court proceedings. He estimates that he has prepared over 400 assessments in the past 20 years pursuant  to s. 211 of the Family Law Act or its predecessor legislation.

[201] Dr. Meuser says he has considerable experience with parental  alienation, estimating that  as many as 25% of his cases have involved  some component  of

same. While he has not attended any  seminars or courses expressly  devoted to alienation, nor has he himself written any  papers or conducted any research in the area, he says he has read extensively  on the subject and is familiar  with both the research and the 'pivotal' publications on the subject.

[202] Dr. Meuser's report is dated April 8, 2014. It comprises 141 pages, unfortunately  without  the benefit of any table of contents.

[203] For the purposes of his report, Dr. Meuser:

- reviewed thousands  of pages of documents  including  many of the affidavits sworn in the litigation between the parties and hundreds  of emails and related documents  forwarded largely by the father;
- listened to more than  15 hours  of covert  audio recordings made primarily by the father during his 'access visits';
- interviewed and undertook formal psychological  testing of both the mother and the father;
- interviewed the three oldest daughters;
- attended each parent's home to observe their interactions with the children  (one observation in the mother's home and three observations in the father's home, the latter because of the complicated parenting routine which disallowed all five children  in the father's home at one time); and,
- conducted phone interviews  with various 'collateral  informants'  identified by the parties.

[204] With respect to the covert audio recordings, Dr. Meuser commented in his report:

The writer notes that literally everyone involved, including the children, at times covertly recorded conversations, parent-child visits, etc. using cell phones. Sadly, this fact underscores the profound and consistent levels of mistrust and animosity which is a prevailing characteristic in this family group

and hints at the roots of their noted troubles in achieving workable and mutually acceptable co-parenting routines.

[205] Large portions of Dr. Meuser's report recite what purport to be interviewee quotes. These quotes are italicized and presented within quotation marks. While Dr. Meuser does not record his interviews,  he says he takes meticulous  notes (over 200 pages in this case) and that the italicized materials are 'direct quotes'.

[206] Dr. Meuser was successfully  challenged  on cross-examination  about the accuracy  of his purported quotes. It quickly  became apparent that many  of the purported quotes were not in fact a verbatim transcript of notes, but rather  had been subjected to some editing.

[207] One telling example put to Dr. Meuser related to his interview  of Jean Bigelow, the principal of the junior  GNS. The quote set out in the report reads:

I felt [T.] w as inappropriately anxious because of [N.G.'s] expressed concerns, and she had a hard time trusting me since I had left her with her Dad years ago.

## [208] Dr. Meuser's notes actually  read:

I felt [T.] was a bit anxious as she had a hard time trusting me since I had let her Dad take her years ago.

The attribution  of T.'s anxiety  to '[N.G.'s]  expressed concerns'  is not found anywhere  in the notes.

[209] As indicated elsewhere in this judgment,  other witnesses have also testified as to the inaccuracies in Dr. Meuser's purported quotes. His notes were not put into evidence and a word for word comparison has not been undertaken.  Nonetheless, the evidence is compelling,  and I am obliged to conclude,  that the purported quotes in the report are not in fact verbatim reporting, but are the product of editing that  has to some extent  imported error and inaccuracy.

[210] Another  major cause for concern  with respect to Dr. Meuser's report relates to his interviews  of the collateral  informants.  The father  identified seven such  persons

to be interviewed and the mother  identified five. Dr. Meuser interviewed all seven  of the father's proposed interviewees,  all but one on April 4, 2014, and only  interviewed one of the five interviewees suggested by the mother (Ms. Elizabeth Hind, who was interviewed on April 6, 2014).

[211] The other four interviewees proposed by the mother all testified at trial. They include her therapist, Dr. Burima; her brother-in-law,  Dr. Hanks; the children's sailing coach, Ms. Moberg-Parker; and the family  physician, Dr. Nielsen. He says he attempted to contact all of these individuals  multiple times on April 6, 7 and 8 (the latter being the date of the actual  report) with the exception of Dr. Burima. With respect to this latter interviewee,  Dr. Meuser stated in his report, 'no number  given', but acknowledged on cross-examination  that he had actually  worked with Dr. Burima in the past and that  her contact information  was readily available, whether  from the College of Psychologists or simply the Internet.  He has no valid excuse for not contacting the one person who might have had the most informed professional insight of all the interviewees into the mother's psychological makeup, namely, her own treating psychologist.

[212] It  appears from Dr. Meuser's testimony  that the mother launched  a complaint against Dr. Meuser with the College of Psychologists and that he acknowledged the section of his report respecting the collateral  interviews was 'compromised'. He says he would  have liked to have had more time with these interviews,  but the 'report had to be done for court',  that he was told it simply 'had to be finished' and therefore, a 'trade-off'  was the result.  Neither the substance  nor outcome of the complaint to the College was put in evidence at trial.

[213] The mother accuses Dr. Meuser of bias. I do not believe such bias exists; however,  there are several aspects of Dr. Meuser's report that could be construed as lending support to the proposition. They  include:

- inappropriate comments on the mother's appearance ('humbly  dressed in ‚Ä¶ frumpy  and aging, loose-fitting [clothes]'  with 'scuffed worn shoes,

oversized wool socks (at times two pairs) and a ponytail'  such that she 'did not match the image one might expect of a paediatric surgeon');

- a 'clinical assessment' of the mother which,  while it is expressly  said to 'fall short of a formal diagnosis', nonetheless  concludes  she has 'significant characteristics synonymous  with ‚Ä¶ several clearly-defined Personality  Disorders', including  Paranoid Personal Disorder and Obsessive-Compulsive Personality  Disorder (the characteristics of which are then recited at length);
- several interviews  and observations of the father were conducted before any interviews  and observations of the mother were made, contrary  to the 'staggered' approach to such  interactions that had been contemplated  by the parties;
- receipt and review of enormous  volumes  of additional, largely  self-serving documentation  from the father during the process (which  was not shared with the mother),  including the latter's identification of the mother's 'mental  health  issues' (including,  perhaps not coincidentally,  ObsessiveCompulsive Personality  Disorder and Paranoid Personality  Disorder), as well as submissions respecting 'extreme  parental alienation' (a phrase used, and conclusion  ultimately  reached, by Dr. Meuser in his report);
- use of language that  does not reflect the objective, dispassionate analysis expected from an expert witness (describing at various points the father's conduct  as 'heroic', 'someone should offer him a medal'; child behaviour that is 'horrendous';  'disturbing' personality  characteristics of the mother who has 'systematically  and willfully  engaged' in a 'toxic, destructive  and systematic pattern of alienation' which  has 'wreaked devastation  in the relationships between [G.G.]  and his oldest two daughters');
- failing to interview  the mother's collateral  witnesses; and

- devoting a substantially  disproportionate part of the report to the recitation of the father's interviews  and collateral  witnesses in comparison to those of the mother.

[214] These matters are all cause for concern and it may very well be that the scheduling  of interviews and the receipt of supplementary  information  from the father has influenced  the conclusions  ultimately  reached by Dr. Meuser. But this does not detract from what is perhaps the most compelling part of his report, namely,  his observations of the children's conduct in the presence of their father. In  his testimony,  he described the behaviour  as 'atrocious' and 'the worst I have ever seen in children'.  In  his report, he described their screaming, shouting,  insults, swearing and expressions of hate for their father. He comments:

The writer will not document every foul and abusive word shouted by these children at their father [G.G.], but suffice it to state that this parent-child observation was without exception the most difficult such observation the writer has conducted in nearly 40 years of professional psychological practice. Never have I seen two young children more hostile and blatantly rude to a parent than [S.] and [T.].

[215] In  the Conclusions  and Recommendations  portion of his report,  Dr. Meuser lists a variety  of criteria often used to identify parental  alienation. Some of the criteria so identified include:

- the child indicating fear and hatred of the targeted parent;
- the child denigrating the targeted parent to friends, family and the public;
- the child creating public scenes involving  untoward  language to embarrass or denigrate the targeted parent;
- the child making false allegations involving  the targeted parent;
- the child displaying severe opposition to contact with the targeted parent;
- the lack of credible or compelling evidence to support the child's usually weak or frivolous  explanations  for the rejection of the targeted parent;

- the targeting parent believing they  are 'protecting'  the child, which  beliefs may develop a delusional or obsessive quality;  and
- the targeting parent voicing support of the targeted parent, yet consistently acting in a manner  that suggests the opposite.

## [216] Having regard to these criteria, Dr. Meuser concludes:

Overwhelmingly, the evidence gleaned during this assessment, from multiple clinical interviews and observations with parents and children, to documented, anecdotal and collateral information from informed sources, points to Parental Alienation  as the root of the noted dysfunction in this family and the ongoing litigation.

[217] Dr. Meuser expresses the opinion that the root cause of the ongoing struggle between the parents respecting custody of and access with the children  'lies in the unshaken  belief by [the mother]  that [the father]  is or has been abusive with the children'.

[218] Rather surprisingly,  Dr. Meuser's report contains very  little commentary  on the specific needs of the five children  and how their present or future  psychological and emotional well-being  is impacted by the alienation he perceives to exist. He simply says:

Allowed to continue unabated, the distress for these bright children might well derail their otherwise brilliant futures ‚Ä¶ From a professional perspective, all children are better off having healthy and supportive relationships with both good parents. Parents who wilfully and without just cause, separate or alienate children from their other parent, if that parent is in fact a 'good' parent, are damaging their children.

## [219]  Dr. Meuser then makes several recommendations:

- the residency and access arrangements  for the three youngest  children should  reflect a more balanced and equal routine  for both parents;
- the father's then-existing  'dysfunctional  access routine'  to the two oldest children  (Thursday  evenings  from 5:00 p.m. to 7:00 p.m.) should  cease immediately; and

- a treatment  program should  be immediately ordered for the two oldest children,  designed specifically to deal with their profound  alienation from their father.

[220] In  his report, Dr. Meuser identified three treatment  programs in British Columbia and the USA which have 'excellent  reputations'  for addressing and repairing parental alienation, namely:

- the 'Family Forward Reunification  Therapy  Program' designed and operated by Alyson Jones in West Vancouver;
- the 'Family Reflections Reunification  Program' operated by Dr. Kathleen Reay in the Okanagan;  and
- the 'Family Bridges: A Workshop for Troubled and Alienated Parent-Child Relationships'  program developed by Drs. Rand and Warshak in the USA.

[221] Dr. Meuser was testifying at trial fully  one year after his s. 211 report had been issued. By that  time, all of his recommendations had been imposed as a result of various court orders. Parenting time was divided on an equal basis and participation in the Alyson  Jones program had been underway  since the early summer of 2014.

[222] Before testifying,  Dr. Meuser had been provided with all of the Alyson  Jones minutes of meetings and progress reports. He observed that Ms. Jones agreed with him that alienation was the issue for this particular  family. He observed that  the mother is the obstacle to progress and that the failure of the Jones program to remediate the family  dysfunction  lies not with the program itself, but rather  with the mother's failure to genuinely  participate.

[223] On cross-examination,  Dr. Meuser was challenged  with the proposition that the children's alienation from their father was in fact estrangement  triggered by the father's conduct.  He acknowledged that violence toward the children  such as pushing  and slapping could certainly  have a significant impact on the children's

attitude and behaviour,  as could poor parenting such as abandoning children to their own devices at challenging  sporting events  or rousing them from bed in the middle of the night  for travel.  He was adamant, however,  that the reaction of the older children  to their father,  as evidenced by his own  observations, the hours of audio recordings, and the reports from third parties, went 'far beyond estrangement'  and could only  be reasonably  explained by persistent parental alienation on the part of the mother.

[224] Dr. Meuser acknowledged that  the children  were 'amazing young  ladies' insofar as their accomplishments  are concerned,  but he noted that it is possible for them to have a crisis in one aspect of their lives (parental relationships)  that does not spill over into other aspects (performance  at school, peer relationships,  etc.).

[225] Dr. Meuser concluded his testimony  by observing that successful  reunification of this family is still possible. He has grave reservations  that the mother's attitude is too entrenched  and that  she will consciously  or unconsciously  undermine  any treatment  program, but he suggests crafting a regime that will somehow compel the mother's participation in the program in a genuine way, failing which there should be resort to one of the alternative  programs which  would involve  the total exclusion  of the mother in parenting of the children until  relationship repair with their father had been sufficiently  established.

## Estrangement and Alienation: Concepts and Possible Remedies

[276] In  high-conflict  separations, both parents commonly  attempt to undermine their children's relationship with the other parent. Occasionally  children  reject one of the parents,  resisting or even refusing contact.  It is in such contexts  that the concepts of 'estrangement'  and 'alienation'  are often invoked.

[277] The difference between an estranged child and an alienated child lies in the cause; estrangement,  sometimes referred to as 'realistic estrangement',  is the label applied when  the child understandably  refuses contact with a parent because of the latter's behaviour whether  it be physical  or emotional abuse, rigid or restrictive parenting,  immature and self-centered  behaviour,  and/or dysfunctional  conduct arising from the parent's own psychological  or psychiatric issues. In such circumstances,  the child's rejection of the parent may well be a reasonable and adaptive response to the estranged parent's behaviour.

[278] An alienated child, on the other hand,  is the label frequently  applied when there is little or no objectively  reasonable cause for the child's rejection of the parent and particularly  when  it is the product of the other parent's hostility and antipathy towards his/her  former spouse and the intentional  undermining  by the former of the child's relationship with the latter. While the undermining  of the relationship can arise from direct or indirect behaviours,  in its worst form  the parental  conduct is expressly designed to alienate the child from the other parent. Such conduct  is pernicious and unpardonable.

[279] Before addressing the discussion of estrangement  and alienation in social science literature,  it may be worthwhile  to set out the advantages  lost by a child who is deprived of positive involvement  by both parents following  separation. In  this case, both Drs. Meuser and Waterman acknowledged that one of the leading scholars in the field of Clinical Child Psychology  is Dr. Joan Kelly. She received her Ph.D. from Yale University  and has worked in the area of child and family issues related to separation and divorce for over 45 years. She has authored many  widely respected publications, including:

- ÔÇ∑ 'Using Child Development Research to Make Appropriate Custody and Access Decisions for Young  Children', Family And Conciliation Courts Review ,  Vol. 28, No. 3, July  2000, pp. 297-311;
- ÔÇ∑ 'Developing Beneficial Parenting Plan Models for Children Following Separation and Divorce', Journal of the American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers ,  Vol. 19, No. 2, (2005);
- ÔÇ∑ 'Parenting After Separation: Addressing Controversies  In A Shifting Landscape', Conference Presentation,  September 29, 2014, Vancouver,  BC.

[280] Some of the relevant  themes emerging from the research described in these and other publications by Dr. Kelly include:

- ÔÇ∑ Positive involvement  of both parents will usually  best address the developmental  needs of young  children  of separated parents;
- ÔÇ∑ Quality  of parenting is a central variable and major predictor of child outcomes;
- ÔÇ∑ Independent  of the positive outcomes of secure attachments  with mothers, secure attachments  with fathers are associated with later positive measures of self-esteem, self-regulation  and compliance, social

competence and peer relationships,  cognitive and verbal skills, I.Q., and emotional and behavioural  adjustment;

- ÔÇ∑ Young  children with involved  fathers  are more likely to be more sociable as infants,  and will have a greater capacity for empathy,  better peer relationships and fewer behavioural  problems;
- ÔÇ∑ As children  with involved  fathers grow older, they are more likely to solve problems better, have higher educational  achievement  and more satisfying adult relationships,  and cope/adapt better to difficult life situations.

[281] Contrast these positive outcomes with the potential consequences to children of long-term  parental alienation described in the testimony  of Dr. Waterman and more particularly  referred to in para. 271 of this judgment,  repeated for ease of reference here:

- ÔÇ∑ problems in school;
- ÔÇ∑ problems and relationships  with peers;
- ÔÇ∑ difficulty  with trust  and relationships later in life;
- ÔÇ∑ difficulties in caring for their own children  in the future;
- ÔÇ∑ mental health  problems such as depression and anxiety,  along with increased risk of alcohol and drug abuse.

[282] Dr. Waterman's observations are supported in part by one of the rare studies into the lives of adult children  of parental alienation: A. Baker, 'The Long-Term Effects Of Parental Alienation On Adult Children:  A Qualitative  Research Study', The American Journal of Family Therapy , 33:289-302, 2005.

[283] There is much  debate whether  parental alienation on the part of a child should  qualify as a psychiatric diagnosis, i.e. a disorder/syndrome  that should  be

recognized as a form of mental illness in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual  of Mental Disorders (DSM-5), Fifth Edition. There are powerful  arguments  that conflicted children,  caught  in the middle of intense and emotionally  charged separation scenarios, should not be labelled as mentally  ill: see C. Houchin et al. , 'The Parental Alienation Debate Belongs In The Courtroom,  Not In  DSM-5,' The Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law ,  Vol. 40, p. 127 (2012).

[284] The term 'parental  alienation syndrome'  was first introduced  in 1985 by Dr. Richard Gardner,  formerly  a psychoanalyst  and child psychiatrist  on the clinical faculty  at Columbia University.  In  the ensuing  decade before his death, Dr. Gardner founded his own publishing  company and published over 30 books (Dr. Meuser brought  one of those books to court during his testimony).  Dr. Gardner was highly sought  as an expert witness and testified in over 400 child custody cases in the United States.

[285] The concept of 'parental  alienation syndrome'  has been controversial  since its inception. A particularly  scathing review of its evidentiary  admissibility and scientific validity can be found in J. Hoult,  'The  Evidentiary Admissibility Of Parental Alienation Syndrome:  Science, Law, And Policy', Children's Legal Rights Journal , Vol. 26, p. 1 (2006).

[286] Dr. Joan Kelly,  referred to above, has been one of the more noteworthy  critics of Dr. Gardner's classification of parental  alienation as a syndrome  or disorder: see J. Kelly &amp; J. Johnston,  'The Alienated Child: A Reformulation  Of Parental  Alienation Syndrome', Fam. Court Rev. 39:249-66, 2001, as well as Dr. Gardner's formal rebuttal  and the Kelly/Johnston  response published in tandem, Family Court Rev. 42:611-2001 and 622-8 (2004).

[287] Regardless of academic debate respecting labels and diagnoses, the simple fact of the matter is that alienation is a useful  and important  concept which is frequently  at play in high-conflict  separations and has been recognized as such in numerous  cases before the Canadian courts.  Arguably  the more important questions revolve  around the remedies and approaches taken by the courts to address

alienation in the best interests of the children involved  and to ensure  the protection of psycho-emotional  well-being in both the short-  and long-term.

[288] A very  helpful  review of the legal responses to alienation can be found  in Bala et al. , 'Alienated Children And Parental Separation: Legal Responses In Canada's Family Courts', 33 Queen's L.J. 79 (2007). The various mechanisms often used by the courts include:

- ÔÇ∑ Detailed case management and parental  conduct orders with cost consequences for non-compliance;
- ÔÇ∑ Judicial exhortation  urging compliance and emphasizing the emotional harm caused to the children  (generally  only  effective  in less severe cases of alienation);
- ÔÇ∑ Court-ordered therapeutic  intervention  where appropriate, while recognizing 'force-marching'  a child to reunification may in some cases be unrealistic  and harmful;
- ÔÇ∑ Ordering supervised access/parenting time to allay any child anxiety  and possibly pave the way for further  strategies to achieve positive relationships;
- ÔÇ∑ Suspension of child or spousal support as a sanction to enforce more engagement with the other parent;
- ÔÇ∑ Transferring  custody from the alienating parent to the rejected parent where expert testimony  establishes the long-term  benefits will outweigh any short-term  emotional trauma  to the child;
- ÔÇ∑ Terminating  access by/parenting  time of the alienated parent when  the alienation is so entrenched  that the 'cure is worse than the illness', recognizing that children  do sometimes resume a relationship  with

a rejected non-custodial  parent after a long period without  contact, albeit perhaps only  in later years.

[289] All of these remedies have been employed by the courts in British Columbia over the years. Recent cases which include detailed discussion of alienation and which exemplify  the breadth and flexibility  of the remedies in the best interests of the children  involved  include:

- ÔÇ∑ I.S. v. D.J.S. ,  2010 BCSC 306;
- ÔÇ∑ L.G. v. R.G. , 2012 BCSC 1365; and
- ÔÇ∑ L.D.K. v. M.A.K. , 2015 BCSC 226.

[290] Some of the observations by Mr. Justice Brown in L.G. v. R.G., supra , resonate in this case:

1. Parental alienation has become a term commonly used in divorce cases to denote the occurrence of breakdown of the relationship between a child and one of the divorcing parents. It most often occurs in cases where the parties have locked themselves into high levels of conflict that often includes custody and access matters.
2. Although alienation of a child from a parent can develop as an inherent side effect of marriage breakdown, deliberate action by a parent, sometimes subtle, sometimes obvious, can also bring it about. Children can become enmeshed in their parents' disputes as parents try to enlist them as a standard-bearer in the parental battles. But young children have not yet developed psychological defences needed to engage in adult disputes and they usually become the first casualty of the wars. It is not surprising that professional literature shows a reliable connection between an increased risk of mental illness and childhood exposure to intense marital conflict battles. Compared with most divorce cases, cases such as these consume a disproportionate number of courtroom days.

## Analysis, Findings and Orders Made

[292] Before setting out my conclusions  on the personalities, parenting styles and past conduct of both parents, it is appropriate to first recite the common-sense observation made by the court in K.D.P. v. A.R.K. , 2011 BCSC 1085:

- [1] No parents are perfect. All have flaws of one kind or another. If families stay together, except in those rare circumstances in which a child is found to be in need of protection, the state and the courts allow imperfect parents to raise their children as best they can. The children, in most cases, are no worse for wear for the experience.
- [2] If families separate, however, and issues of custody and access arise, in the guise of determining the best interests of the child, a parent's flaws of character and conduct are put under a microscope. In such circumstances, care must be taken not to lose sight of the strengths that a party brings to the challenge of raising a child.

[293] In  high-conflict  matrimonial litigation, such  as the case at bar, the adversarial system often serves to only increase the acrimony between the parties. People who once loved each other exchange  vile insults  and attach nasty  labels to perceived deficiencies in character.  Friends, family,  police, teachers and ministry  officials are summoned to denigrate one parent and advocate on behalf  of the other. The interests of the children,  best served by the positive involvement  of both parents, are sacrificed on the altar of self-centered  animosity.

[294] All of these things have occurred in this case.

[295] The mother is a widely respected pediatric surgeon,  a profession dedicated to advancing the health  and well-being  of children.  Yet,  for the purpose of this litigation, the father calls her 'a Nazi', 'a beast', 'bizarre',  'violent'  'aggressive' and 'mentally  ill'.

[296] The father,  who is well-educated  and highly  accomplished in the world of finance and who clearly  loves his children,  is labelled by the mother as a violent  child abuser who locks children  in cages and throws them downstairs.  Relatively innocuous  'incidents'  spanning more than  a decade in some instances  are dredged up to establish a 'pattern'  of indifference and incompetency.

[297] There is little doubt neither the mother nor the father  is a perfect parent,  nor will they be in the future.  They  both have flaws. They  have both been guilty  of poor judgment  and unpleasant  conduct  in the past and they  will doubtless be guilty  of the same in the future.  Notwithstanding  such  imperfections, however,  I wish to make it perfectly  clear that the mother is not 'mentally  ill' and the father is not 'a violent  child abuser'. Both parents require an immediate attitude adjustment  in this regard.

[302] During his testimony,  and in the history he recited to Dr. Meuser, the father paints himself as being dominated throughout  his marriage by an obsessive, controlling  woman,  who he now labels 'violent'  and 'mentally  ill', and who he says was verbally  and physically  abusing the children over many years. The latter  is in reference to frequent  swearing by the mother and to disciplinary measures that included spanking and 'time-outs'  in the garage. However,  he acknowledges that he too put the children  in the garage for punishment  and that he too is subject  to a flaring temper which  has led to such things as smashing a water pitcher on the floor in front of the children,  or, more recently,  slapping S. in the face at the airport in response to misbehaviour  or acts of defiance.

[303] I  find as a fact that these incidents did occur as did the various incidents of poor judgment  described by the disinterested non-family  witnesses recounted  earlier in this judgment.

[304] One cannot help but wonder whether  the father's own self-described family history  of toxic dysfunction  and long-term  estrangement  from his parents at an early age might have affected his psychological  make-up and approach to parenting. It  must be acknowledged, however,  that  the father  accepts at least partial responsibility for the present family dysfunction  and by all accounts  has complied with and benefited from the counselling  he has received, whether  from Alyson Jones or others. It is my sincere hope the father will continue  to seek out the benefits of such counselling  and that his parental skills will improve accordingly.

[305] When posed a question during his examination in chief, the father would sometimes provide a rambling monologue  of events  as he saw them replete with tears, melodramatic exaggeration and statements  of pride at finally  'escaping' the control of the mother.  I treat his characterization  of various events  with  caution.

However, I accept as genuine his denial of locking T. in a dog kennel or pushing S. down stairs and I find as a fact that such incidents did not actually  occur.

[306] The mother's presentation in court was much  less dramatic and flamboyant. At times she displayed an unemotional,  flat effect. She was, of course, selfrepresented at this trial and, it must be said, performed remarkably  well in difficult circumstances.  She was organized and thorough.  Her arguments  were well-written and logical.

[307] It  was also apparent from the mother's presentation and evidence that she is a demanding person who insists on disciplined conduct and prefers detailed rules and schedules  over more informal  or free-wheeling  approaches to most matters. This no doubt serves her well in her profession but it has also resulted in a certain inflexibility  and lack of cooperation which  has substantially  contributed to the parenting disputes and multiple  court attendances  in this case.

[308] In  her oral submissions the mother emphasized that 'I believe my kids'. This includes everything  they  tell her, whether  it be the father pushing  children down stairs or locking them in dog kennels.  She insists that the views of the children  have not been properly heard in this case and states 'If  I don't do it, who will?'

[309] Dr. Meuser expresses the view that 'the root of the ongoing struggle  between N.G. and G.G. over access with their children  lies in the unshaken  belief by [the mother]  that [the father]  is or has been abusive with the children'.  I accept that this is very  likely the case. As indicated earlier, however,  the various instances of poor judgment  or poor parenting  skills by the father do not amount  to abuse of the children  and there is no proper basis for the mother's belief that child protection is warranted.  As stated before, attitude adjustment  on her part is required.

[310] This, then,  leads to the question which  lies at the heart of this case, namely, alienation.

[311] The evidence clearly establishes that the two eldest children  have become increasingly  alienated from their father.  I agree with both Dr. Meuser and Alyson

Jones that the level of antipathy  displayed by the eldest children,  both verbally  and in their conduct,  is not likely the result solely  of the father's occasional poor judgment or lapses in parenting  skills. There is no doubt, and I find as a fact, that the alienation has also been caused in very  large measure by the conduct of the mother. It  is important  to note there is virtually  no evidence in this trial respecting the private communications between mother and children  and so not all of the mother's alienation behaviours  can be readily identified. Nevertheless,  her alienating conduct  includes:

- ÔÇ∑ leaving the home and removing the children from the reach of their father without  prior discussion or notice;
- ÔÇ∑ effectively  preventing  the father from having  any meaningful  access to the older children  throughout  2013;
- ÔÇ∑ resisting and objecting to all attempts by the father to increase access to and parenting time with the children,  frequently  invoking  the unwarranted specter of child abuse in that regard;
- ÔÇ∑ organizing and insisting upon excessive child activities involving unmanageably  complex logistics invariably  conflicting  or rendering more difficult the father's parenting time with the children;
- ÔÇ∑ paying lip service to but in actuality  resisting and sabotaging therapies specifically designed to encourage family reunification  and equally involved  parenting time (Alyson  Jones);
- ÔÇ∑ multiple  reporting of 'incidents' to the police or ministry (or to others with the design of having  same so reported) in an effort to substantiate opposition to granting  or increasing the father  parenting time (the scratching incident of January  2015 being the most recent example);
- ÔÇ∑ coaching the children  for their interviews  by the Ministry  (and likely others as well);  and

- ÔÇ∑ keeping the children  informed, and indeed involved  as a witness in one instance,  of her version of the high-conflict  court proceedings thereby inducing worry  and distress designed to reinforce alignment  with the mother.

[312] Long-term alienation of a child from his or her parent can lead to significant psychological  harm of the sort described earlier in this judgment.  The conduct on the part of a parent expressly  intended to cause or perpetuate alienation is wrong and completely  unacceptable  by any standards.

[313] Quite apart from such intended design, in high-conflict  situations even young  children  can and will pick up on unconsciously  telegraphed messages, i.e. emotionless or negative expression when  speaking about the other parent, flat or negative reactions to the other parent's activities with the children,  failing to encourage the children to look forward to parenting time with the other parent, failing to remind the children of the other parent's positive attributes,  and the like. There  is little  or no evidence in this particular case regarding such  matters but it seems highly likely both parents are guilty  of same.

## Best Interests of the Children and Orders Made

[314] Section 16 of the Divorce Act addresses custody  orders and makes it mandatory for the court:

- ÔÇ∑ to consider only  the best interests of the children  as determined by reference to the condition, means, needs and other circumstances of the children;
- ÔÇ∑ not to consider the past conduct of any person unless  it is relevant  to the ability of that person to act as a parent;
- ÔÇ∑ to give effect to the 'principle' that  children should  have as much  contact with each parent as is consistent  with the best interests of the children; and

- ÔÇ∑ for that purpose, to consider the willingness  of the parents to facilitate such contact.

[315]  The Family Law Act contains almost identical mandates, however,  it specifies additional considerations to be taken into account.  Sections 37 and 38 of that Act provide as follows:

## Best interests of child

- 37 (1) In  making an agreement or order under this Part respecting guardianship, parenting arrangements or contact with a child, the parties and the court must consider the best interests of the child only.
- (2) To determine what is in the best interests of a child, all of the child's needs and circumstances must be considered, including the following:
- (a) the child's health and emotional well-being;
- (b) the child's views, unless it would be inappropriate to consider them;
- (c) the nature and strength of the relationships between the child and significant persons in the child's life;
- (d) the history of the child's care;
- (e) the child's need for stability, given the child's age and stage of development;
- (f) the ability  of each person who is a guardian or seeks guardianship of the child, or who has or seeks parental responsibilities, parenting time or contact with the child, to exercise his or her responsibilities;
- (g) the impact of any family violence on the child's safety, security or well-being, whether the family violence is directed toward the child or another family member;
- (h) whether the actions of a person responsible for family violence indicate that the person may be impaired in his or her ability to care for the child and meet the child's needs;
- (i) the appropriateness of an arrangement that would require the child's guardians to cooperate on issues affecting the child, including whether requiring cooperation would increase any risks to the safety, security or well-being of the child or other family members;
- (j) any civil or criminal proceeding relevant to the child's safety, security or well-being.
- (3) An agreement or order is not in the best interests of a child unless it protects, to the greatest extent possible, the child's physical, psychological and emotional safety, security and well-being.

- (4) In making an order under this Part, a court may consider a person's conduct only if it substantially affects a factor set out in subsection (2), and only to the extent that it affects that factor.

## Assessing family  violence

- 38 For the purposes of section 37 (2) (g) and (h) [best interests of child] , a court must consider all of the following:
- (a) the nature and seriousness of the family violence;
- (b) how recently the family violence occurred;
- (c) the frequency of the family violence;
- (d) whether any psychological or emotional abuse constitutes, or is evidence of, a pattern of coercive and controlling behaviour directed at a family member;
- (e) whether the family violence was directed toward the child;
- (f) whether the child was exposed to family violence that was not directed toward the child;
- (g) the harm to the child's physical, psychological and emotional safety, security and well-being as a result of the family violence;
- (h) any steps the person responsible for the family violence has taken to prevent further family violence from occurring;
- (i) any other relevant matter.

[316] In  this case both parents are highly  educated, accomplished individuals. Both clearly  love their children  and wish to protect  and promote the children's physical and psycho-emotional  well-being.  While both parents have markedly different personalities and parenting styles,  they each bring valuable  attributes to the parenting equation which will benefit the children  both now and in the future.

[317] In  this case neither  parent would dispute the positive outcomes of secure attachments  by children  with both their mother and their father assuming both parents are well-adjusted,  capable individuals.  Neither parent would dispute that children  in such  circumstances will likely have positive self-esteem,  greater social competence and peer relationships,  and enhanced  maturity  to cope with difficult  life situations in the future.

[318] The difficulty  in the present case arises from the antipathy  each parent bears towards the other and the alienation from the father  of the two older children.  The overarching  question is how to best address the circumstances  in the best interests

of all five children and in a manner that will protect and promote their long-term psycho-emotional  development  and well-being.

[319] To a large extent,  the court has already addressed the question; in addition to ordering joint guardianship,  it has also adopted the key recommendations  made by Dr. Meuser in his s. 211 report. In  particular,  the court has:

- ÔÇ∑ ordered the parents and the older children  to participate in a treatment program geared towards resolution  of the alienation problem (Alyson Jones);
- ÔÇ∑ required the parents to follow the reasonable recommendations  made by the therapists in that  program; and
- ÔÇ∑ provided for equal parenting time on a week-on, week-off basis for all the children.

[320] The more pointed question is whether  these measures have been successful and, if not, what is now to be done in the circumstances.

[321] The father says the Alyson  Jones program has not been successful,  that the mother continues  to engage in alienating behaviours  designed to frustrate  any relationship with his daughters,  and that  accordingly more drastic measures are required. He seeks to terminate the Alyson  Jones program and instead implement one of the two other programs advocated by Dr. Meuser,  namely,  Dr. Reay's 'Family Reflections  Reunification  Program' in the Okanagan  or the 'family  bridges' program developed by Drs. [323] In  my view, many of the problems encountered  by the parties are caused by the hectic schedule of activities, the logistics of which  are far too challenging. [324] The mother seems to respect discipline and order. I intend to impose both at this time. She assured the Court that she would comply.  We will see.

[325] I  have authored  these admittedly  lengthy  reasons not just for the benefit of the parties but also for the consumption  of their therapists and counsellors.  The children  may react negatively,  at least initially,  to the cancellation  of activities which they  appear to enjoy,  particularly  sailing. ## CHILD SUPPORT

[330] In Bennett v. Bennett , 2015 BCSC 699, I had occasion to set out for the benefit of an unrepresented  party a simple and non-exhaustive  summary  of basic legal principles applicable to the awarding of child support by the courts. In  this case, the unrepresented  mother  is an intelligent,  sophisticated individual  who likely has a better understanding  of the issues than the unrepresented  parent in the Bennett case. Nonetheless  it may be helpful  for both parties if I repeat the Bennett summary here:

## GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF CHILD SUPPORT

...

## Obligation to Pay Child Support

- [40] Three fundamental principles apply to all child support applications:
1. parents have a joint and ongoing legal obligation to support their children;
2. it is the children, not the custodial parent, who have the right to support; and
3. support payments are based on earning capacity and not just on what a parent actually earns; as a result, a parent has a legal obligation to earn as much as he or she is reasonably capable of earning so the children can receive an appropriate level of support.
- [41] The court has jurisdiction under both the federal Divorce Act , R.S.C. 1985, c. 3 (2nd Supp.), and the provincial Family Law Act , S.B.C. 2011, c. 25, to award support. Subject to certain exceptions, both Acts require that the amount of child support in any given case be governed by the Guidelines . These Guidelines are regulations which have been made under the Divorce Act . They include tables setting the amount of child support based on the incomes of the parents. They also provide for payment of 'special or extraordinary expenses', undue hardship, variation of support orders, how a parent's income is determined, imputation of income in certain circumstances, and exchange of financial information.

...

- [43] The court can issue orders requiring the payment of child support prospectively or retroactively. It also has the power to vary, rescind or suspend orders that have already been made, although the circumstances warranting any variation will be governed by the Guidelines .
- [44] When exercising its discretion to make a retroactive child support order, the court will consider various factors including:

- ÔÇ∑ the circumstances surrounding any delay in seeking a court order for support;
- ÔÇ∑ whether the parent has engaged in blameworthy conduct;
- ÔÇ∑ the past and present circumstances of the children, including their needs and standard of living; and
- ÔÇ∑ whether any hardship would be caused by a retroactive award.

[45] Generally speaking, the child support obligation fluctuates with the level of income available to the parents. The latter have an obligation to support their children in a way that is commensurate with their income. A payor parent who does not increase child support payments to correspond with increased income does not fulfill his or her obligation to the children.

- [46] Parents are obliged to fully and honestly disclose income information and documentation. At a minimum, this includes tax returns, notices of assessment/reassessment, statements of earnings, financial statements for self-employment, partnership or incorporated enterprises, and the like. Failure to provide disclosure can result in adverse inferences against, and the imputation of income to, the non-disclosing parent as the court considers appropriate.

## Determining Income

- [47] The Guidelines set out specific procedures to determine the annual income of a parent. The starting point is the parent's 'total income' disclosed in his or her income tax return. Schedule III  of the Guidelines then sets out various adjustments that can be made to that figure, whether as deductions or add-backs.
- [48] If there has been fluctuation in income or non-recurring receipts/expenses in the previous three years such that the number referred to above might not be the fairest determination of a parent's income, the court has a discretion to determine an annual amount that would be fair and reasonable in the circumstances.
- [49] Where a parent is a shareholder, director or officer of a corporation and the court is of the opinion that the parent's annual income set out on his or her tax return does not fairly reflect all the money available to that person for the payment of child support, the court has the discretion to include all or part of the corporation's pre-tax income in the analysis. Unreasonable payments or benefits paid to 'non-arm's-length' persons dealing with the corporation can be added back to that pre-tax income.
- [50] Lastly, the court has the ability to impute income to a parent in a wide variety of circumstances. These include such things as intentional underemployment or unemployment, diversion of income to affect the level of child support, unreasonable deduction of expenses, failure to provide income information, and the like.

## Determining Quantum in Shared Custody Cases

- [51] Very often one parent will have custody of the children for most of the time. In such situations, the focus is on the income of the other 'payor' parent

and the amount he or she is required to pay to the 'custodial' parent in accordance with the Guidelines .

[52] In cases of 'split custody' where each parent has custody of one or more children, the amount of a child support order is the difference between the amount each parent would otherwise pay if a child support order was sought against each of the parents (s. 8 of the Guidelines ).

[53] Sometimes parents have 'shared custody', which is defined by s. 9 of the Guidelines to mean each parent having access to/custody of a child for at least 40% of the time over the course of a year. This is the situation in the present case, i.e. shared custody. In such cases. s. 9 of the Guidelines requires any child support order to take into account:

- (a) the amount set out in the applicable Guideline tables for each of the parents (i.e., essentially determining a 'set-off' figure);
- (b) the increased cost of shared custody arrangements; and
- (c) the conditions, means, needs and other circumstances of each spouse and of any child for whom support is sought.

[54] These factors emphasize flexibility  and fairness. The 'set-off' of each parent's income is simply a starting point and is not the end of the inquiry. The factors identified in (b) and (c) are equally important elements. They provide flexibility  to ensure that the economic reality and particular circumstances of each family are accounted for and that the level of child support is fair.

[55] The words 'conditions, means, needs and other circumstances' import expansive inquiry. ...

[56] The 'basic' or 'table' amount of support under the Guidelines is designed to provide contribution towards basic expenses for children such as food, housing, transportation, clothing, personal care, allowances, and vacation. However, the court also has the power to provide additional support amounts to cover certain identified 'special or extraordinary expenses'.

[57] 'Special' expenses include things like necessary childcare, healthcare costs, and post-secondary education.

[58] Section 7 of the Guidelines also identifies two types of 'extraordinary expenses'. One category is for primary/secondary school education or for any other educational programs that meet the children's particular needs. The other is for 'extracurricular activities'.

[59] The term 'extraordinary expense' is defined by the Guidelines . It includes expenses which the parent cannot reasonably cover having regard to his or her income and the basic child support payment. It also includes other expenses appropriate to such things as the special needs and talents of the children, the nature of the program/activities, overall cost, and the like.

[60] There are two overriding factors to be considered in determining whether an expense qualifies for additional support under s. 7 of the Guidelines , namely, that the expense be:

1. necessary in relation to the child's best interests; and
2. reasonable in relation to the means of the parents and to the family's spending pattern before separation.

[61] With respect to all such special or extraordinary expenses, the general guiding principle is that they are to be shared by the parents in proportion to their respective incomes after deducting any contribution from the child.

## Father's Income

[337] The simple fact of the matter is that for the past three years the father has had zero income except for universal  childcare benefits of $2,500 in 2012 and income assistance ('welfare')  in the amount  of $11,500 in 2013. The  question is what amount of income, if any,  should be imputed to the father  on account of him being 'intentionally  under-employed  or unemployed'  within the meaning of s. 19(1)(a) of the Child Support Guidelines .

[338] This section does not import any requirement  of bad faith. A parent will be considered intentionally  under-employed  or unemployed  if that parent is earning less

than  he or she is capable of earning having  regard to his or her age, education, experience, skills and health,  as well as availability of work, ability to relocate, and the like.

[339] The father is 53 years old. There is no evidence of any  health  problems on his part. He is extremely  well-educated  and has both qualifications and work experience that would ordinarily  indicate an ability to be employed in the banking or financial industries. He has expertise in construction  and property development  projects. He formerly  earned significant amounts  of money both from his employment  at Riverdale International  Inc.  as well as his own property development  portfolio.

[340] It  is apparent that the father has maintained a relationship with persons formerly  associated with Riverdale International.  His form F8 financial  statement filed December 22, 2014 attaches a September 10, 2013 letter from Mr. Jaafar Jalabi, the principal of an enterprise headquartered in Madrid, Spain and called 'Parcelatoria de Gonzalo  Chacon, S.A.'. Mr. Jalabi has paid for all expenses associated with flying  the father back and forth to New York for the purposes of that undertaking  which,  according to the father, did not come to fruition.

[341] It  is also apparent that the father has prepared or assisted in the preparation of 'final accounting'  documents  in respect of a 'HY  Realty  and PGC Service NY1' project in the fall 2014. Indeed, according to documents  put into evidence at trial, it appears that the father even had an email address with PGC ('[grg]@parscelatoria.com').

[342] In  his submissions, counsel for the father  was careful  to say that for the year 2014, the father  'reported having  no income for that year'.  However,  there is simply no evidence before the Court which contradicts the father's testimony  that he has received no income from PGC or indeed any other source in 2014-15.

[343] The evidence of both the father and his mother is to the effect that  he has been borrowing monies from family  and friends to meet his ongoing household expenses as well as to pay his costs of the Alyson Jones therapy and his legal fees. The amount provided to him by his mother to date is in excess of $150,000.

[344] In  his affidavit sworn January  26, 2015 and filed the next  day for the purposes of one of the various  court applications in this case, the father  stated that, among other things,  (paraphrasing except where indicated otherwise):

[359] Parenting time of the children  has been equally  shared by the mother and father since the court order to that effect made July  4, 2014. ## SPOUSAL SUPPORT

[363] The husband seeks an order for spousal support pursuant  to s. 15.2 of the Divorce Act , including retroactive  support from March 1, 2013, being approximately one month following  the date the mother moved from the former family  residence with the children.

## BASIC PRINCIPLES OF SPOUSAL SUPPORT

...

- [54] Spousal support is a creature of statute: both the Divorce Act [section 15.2] and the Family Law Act [part 7, division 4] empower the court to require one spouse to pay support monies to the other. Both statutes leave a broad discretion to judges in individual cases for all three dimensions of spousal support, namely: (1) entitlement, (2) duration, and (3) amount.
- [55] The threshold question is entitlement. This is determined with reference to certain 'objectives' set out in both statutes in almost identical language. Both statutes mandate that these objectives be taken into account. The objectives include:
3. ÔÇ∑ recognition of any economic advantages or disadvantages to the spouses arising from the relationship between them or its breakdown;
4. ÔÇ∑ apportionment between the spouses of any financial consequences arising from the care of their children, beyond the duty to provide support for the children;
5. ÔÇ∑ relieving any economic hardship of the spouses arising from the breakdown of their relationship; and
6. ÔÇ∑ as far as practicable, promoting the economic self-sufficiency of each spouse within a reasonable period of time.
- [56] The leading decisions from the Supreme Court of Canada dealing with this subject have articulated three separate bases for entitlement to

spousal support: compensatory, non-compensatory, and contractual support: Moge v. Moge , [1992] 3 S.C.R. 813 (compensatory support); Bracklow v. Bracklow , [1999] 1 S.C.R. 420 (non-compensatory support); and Miglin v. Miglin , 2003 SCC 24 (contractual support).

[57] Compensatory principles address the contributions of a spouse to the relationship and the spouse's losses upon its breakdown. The classic application, although by no means the only one, arises in relationships where one spouse did not pursue employment outside the home in order to care for the children. The end of that relationship places the spouse at an economic disadvantage, which compensatory support should address ('foregone careers and missed opportunities': Bracklow ).

- [58] Non-compensatory principles address the needs of a spouse after the breakdown of the relationship. This kind of support arises out of the relationship itself and is based on the social obligation of a spouse to look after a former partner who is in difficulty, rather than letting that burden fall upon the public.
- [59] Self-sufficiency is only one objective, and is only a goal 'insofar as practicable'. In determining what is practicable, the court will consider all circumstances, including the recipient spouse's age, gender, skills, education, opportunity for retraining, and the realistic prospect of the spouse finding a job that enables him or her to become self-sufficient: Tedham v. Tedham , 2005 BCCA 502.
- [60] However, no one principle or set of objectives dominates the analysis. A court should balance the various relevant factors and strike the balance that best achieves justice in the particular case. This is a highly fact-specific exercise in each case.
- [61] Marriage alone does not automatically entitle a spouse to support upon its dissolution. Further, no single statutory objective is paramount, so both the compensatory and non-compensatory grounds can be used to justify entitlement. Generally speaking, however, any significant disparity in incomes [*or standards of living] at the end of a marriage or relationship will usually give rise to an entitlement to some spousal support for some period of time.
- [62] The leading case law referred to above addressed only entitlement and not the more mundane and difficult issues of amount and duration with which the trial courts are called upon to grapple on a daily basis.
- [63] Under the Family Law Act , if entitlement to spousal support is established having regard to the objectives set forth above, then s. 160 of the Act makes it mandatory for the other spouse to provide support. The amount and duration of that support must then be determined on a consideration of the conditions, means, needs and other circumstances of each spouse including the three circumstances specifically identified in s. 162 of the Act . These circumstances are identical to the three 'factors' also specified as mandatory considerations under s. 15(4) of the Divorce Act and include:
7. ÔÇ∑ the length of time the spouses live together;
8. ÔÇ∑ the functions performed by each spouse during the period they lived together; and

- ÔÇ∑ any agreement between the parties or any order relating to the support of either spouse.

[64] Spousal misconduct is not a factor to be considered in awarding spousal support except to the extent conduct arbitrarily or unreasonably (a) causes, prolongs or aggravates the need for spousal support, or (b) affects the ability to provide spousal support [s. 166, FLA ].

[65] In practice, the Federal Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines released by the Federal Department of Justice are usually applied in most cases to determine the amount and duration of spousal support, whether under the Divorce Act or as a 'useful tool' under the Family Law Act . While these Guidelines are only 'advisory' in nature, British Columbia is one of the provinces which has strongly endorsed their use: Yemchuk v. Yemchuk , 2005 BCCA 406, Chutter v. Chutter , 2008 BCCA 507.

- [66] The Guidelines provide two basic formulas for determining the amount and duration of spousal support. The first is the 'without child support formula' and the second is the 'with child support formula'. As the names suggest, the dividing line between the two is the absence or presence of a dependent child and a concurrent child support obligation at the time spousal support is determined.

[67] Both formulas use income sharing as the method for determining the amount of spousal support. The formulas then produce ranges for the amount and duration of support, not just a single number. The precise number chosen within that range will be determined by the court depending upon the facts of any particular case.

[68] The starting point under both formulas is the definition of income used in the Federal Child Support Guidelines . Generally speaking a spouse's annual income is determined using the sources of income set out under the heading 'total income' and aggregated on line 150 of an individual's income tax return. The Guidelines also specify certain adjustments which can then be taken into account. ...

- [69] Finally,  the Guidelines provide for the concept of imputing income to a spouse in a wide variety of appropriate circumstances including such things as:
2. ÔÇ∑ a spouse being intentionally underemployed or unemployed;
3. ÔÇ∑ diversion of income;
4. ÔÇ∑ tax exemptions;
5. ÔÇ∑ unreasonable deduction of expenses from income;
6. ÔÇ∑ failing  to reasonably utilize property to generate income; and
7. ÔÇ∑ failing  to provide income information when under a legal obligation to do so.

[*added at this time]
