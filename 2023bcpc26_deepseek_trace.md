LEGAL REASONING TRACE
JURISDICTIONAL SCRUTINY: This interim application is heard in the Provincial Court of British Columbia (BCPC) within a protracted parenting and support trial. The BCPC has clear jurisdiction under the Family Law Act to make case management orders, including those related to obtaining children's views and appointing counsel under ss. 202 and 203. While the BCPC cannot create binding precedent, Judge Doulis demonstrates a sophisticated, precedent-aware analysis, meticulously applying BCCA and BCSC authorities to the statutory framework. The decision is procedurally sound and falls squarely within the court's mandate to manage trials and ensure all relevant best interests factors, particularly the children's views, are properly before the court.

STATUTORY SUBSUMPTION: The application is a masterclass in applying the FLA's child-participation provisions. The judge anchors the entire analysis in the paramountcy of the child's best interests under s. 37. The specific issue—how to obtain the children's views—is governed by s. 37(2)(b), which mandates consideration of those views unless inappropriate. The judge correctly notes the weight of views increases with age and maturity [para 81]. The mother sought an order under s. 203 (appointment of counsel for child). The judge correctly identifies the two-part statutory test: (1) the degree of conflict is so severe it significantly impairs the parties' capacity to act in the child's best interests, and (2) appointment is necessary to protect the child's best interests [para 90]. The judge also considers the alternative mechanisms under s. 202 (hearsay evidence/directions) and s. 211 (views of the child report). The factual findings—extreme parental conflict, a dated and potentially trauma-influenced s. 211 report, and the children's reluctance to see their father—are meticulously mapped onto these statutory options to justify the chosen remedy.

PRECEDENTIAL ALIGNMENT: The judgment is deeply aligned with binding and persuasive precedent. The cornerstone is J.E.S.D. v. Y.E.P., 2018 BCCA 286, which provides the authoritative interpretation of s. 203. The judge correctly applies J.E.S.D., noting that a child's wishes are a factor but not determinative of best interests, and that the UNCRC does not override the statutory conditions [paras 92-94]. The analysis is fortified by a survey of lower court decisions (K.J.C., M.C., A.W., Goldsmith, Clayton, S.C., S.T.C., D.R.), demonstrating a nuanced understanding of when each tool (s. 211 report, judicial interview, or s. 203 counsel) is appropriate. The judge distinguishes cases where counsel was denied (e.g., Clayton, where a s. 211 report was ordered instead) and aligns with those where it was granted due to severe conflict and the need for an independent child voice (e.g., S.T.C., D.R.). There is no precedent gap; the reasoning synthesizes the case law to support a discretionary order tailored to the specific, high-conflict facts.

ARGUMENT STRENGTH: The mother's application succeeded on the doctrine of "Severe Parental Conflict Impairing Best Interests Analysis." Her argument was persuasive because it was evidence-based, pointing to the long, acrimonious litigation history, the schoolyard assault, mutual protection orders, and the children's polarized views as captured in the 2021 s. 211 report [paras 43, 61-63]. This evidence concretely satisfied the J.E.S.D. test for s. 203: the conflict was so severe it impaired both parents' objectivity. The father's opposition, by implication, failed because it could not rebut the overwhelming evidence of conflict or the necessity of an independent mechanism to ascertain the children's current, un-influenced views. The judge found the existing s. 211 report was outdated and potentially skewed by a recent traumatic event, and that a new report would cause undue trial delay [para 112].

JUDICIAL SENTIMENT: The judgment reflects a tone of pragmatic concern and protective intervention, not disdain for the parties but for the situation's impact on the children. The judge explicitly notes the corrosive effect of the conflict: "Given the intensity of the conflict between the parents, I do not consider the parents' evidence as to what the children have told them sufficiently reliable to give it significant probative weight. In high conflict cases, Children will often say what they believe will appease the parent who is present" [para 111(d)]. This shows judicial skepticism of the parents' narratives and a determination to shield the children from being pawns. The reference to the traumatic school incident informing the children's earlier views [para 111(e)] further demonstrates a careful, child-centric approach to evaluating evidence.

IRAC/CoT SYNTHESIS
• ISSUE: Should the court appoint legal counsel for the two children (ages 14 and 10) pursuant to FLA s. 203, or pursue an alternative method (updated s. 211 report or judicial interview) to obtain their views on parenting arrangements?
• RULES: The governing rule is FLA s. 37 (best interests). The specific mechanism for obtaining a child's views is discretionary, with options under ss. 202, 203, and 211. The test for appointing counsel under s. 203 is conjunctive: (i) severe conflict impairing parental capacity to act in the child's best interests, and (ii) necessity to protect the child's best interests. Precedent (J.E.S.D.) holds that a child's wishes are relevant but not determinative.
• APPLICATION: Applying the s. 203 test, the judge finds the first condition met based on the "severe" conflict evidenced by five years of litigation, allegations of alienation, a physical altercation between the mother and father's new partner, and reciprocal protection orders [paras 43, 63, 111]. This conflict impairs both parents' ability to objectively present the children's best interests. The second condition is met because the existing s. 211 report is 18 months old and was conducted soon after a traumatic event, compromising its currency and neutrality [para 111(e)]. A new s. 211 report would cause unacceptable trial delay [para 112]. A judicial interview, while possible, is less comprehensive than having a counsel who can investigate and advocate for the children's interests throughout the remainder of the trial [para 113-114]. Therefore, appointing counsel under s. 203 is "necessary to protect the Children's best interests" [para 115].
• CONCLUSION: The application is granted. Counsel from the Child and Youth Legal Centre is appointed for both children with broad powers to investigate, communicate with the children, and make submissions to the court.
